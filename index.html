Objective-Aware Sequence Modelling for Drift Forecasting and Anomaly Detection in Quantum Hardware

We recast superconducting-qubit reliability as a multivariate, mean-reverting forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem and benchmark four sequence-model families under one leakage-free protocol: coherence is genuinely forecastable, and the best detector is objective-dependent — there is no universal winner. Background & Motivation

Why qubit-parameter drift is a sequence-modelling problem

Superconducting processors are not static. Relaxation time $T_1$, dephasing time $T_2$, single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and tunable-coupling parameters all evolve continuously under temperature, flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history. This is more than a nuisance: in the NISQ regime it caps usable circuit depth, and in the fault-tolerant regime it threatens the assumption that physical error rates stay below threshold long enough for error correction to suppress logical errors.

At hundred- to thousand-qubit scale, deciding when a device has drifted out of specification and which qubits or intervals warrant recalibration is a data problem beyond manual inspection. The calibration stream is a multivariate stochastic process with slow periodic structure, secular degradation and stochastic fluctuation — forecasting it and flagging off-manifold windows maps cleanly onto modern time-series learning. Yet the literature offers little decision-oriented guidance: which model family should an operations pipeline deploy, at what parameter cost, for which monitoring objective? That is the gap we address.

Tutorial & Background

How the reliability problem becomes a benchmark

The modelling pipeline is deliberately simple to audit: simulate or load telemetry, split it chronologically so the future never leaks into training, fit forecasting or anomaly models, and report the metric that matches the operational decision. Forecast error answers "how close is the next calibration window?" while ROC-AUC and recall answer "which intervals should be escalated first?"

The statistical interpretation is as important as the model choice. The page separates multi-seed quantities from single-run point estimates, reports confidence intervals where available, and avoids presenting one architecture as universally best. GRU is the best incident-aware generalist in the reported comparison, Transformer is a periodic anomaly-ranking specialist, and rank-1 reconstruction exposes drift as an off-manifold event.

Contributions & Novelty

Exactly what is new

The manuscript states three contributions; each is reproducible end-to-end on commodity CPU hardware.

  1. A leakage-free, objective-aware benchmark. We formalise quantum-hardware reliability as a joint forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem over a physically motivated, reproducible telemetry generator, and release one leakage-free benchmark that evaluates four sequence-model families on this task and on three heterogeneous real-world regimes under a single protocol.
  2. Model selection must be objective-aware. We show empirically that the GRU is a robust, parameter-efficient generalist — the only model to achieve non-trivial incident detection — whereas the Transformer is a specialist that excels on periodic calibration-like signals but fails to generalise. No single architecture wins everywhere.
  3. A reconstruction detector with a provable knob. We introduce a low-rank reconstruction-based drift detector and identify the bottleneck rank as the control knob separating drift from nominal behaviour, yielding an interpretable, training-free-at-inference monitor backed by an empirical-optimality theorem.
Mathematics & Method

The model, the estimators, and why they work

Everything below is a deterministic function of integer seeds. The two theorems explain the two headline empirics: why forecast skill grows with horizon, and why detection is strongest at low reconstruction rank.

Drift generator (physics)

A mean-reverting multivariate telemetry process

For qubit $q$ at time $t$ (hours, $t = \text{step}\times 0.5$) the relaxation time superposes a slow qubit-dependent oscillation (48-hour recalibration-like cycle), secular ageing, and Gaussian fluctuation:

$$ T_1(q,t) = \max\!\Big(10,\; 80 + 20\sin\!\big(\tfrac{2\pi t}{48} + 0.8q\big) - 0.05\,t + \varepsilon\Big), \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2^2). $$

The dephasing time tracks $T_1$ via $T_2 = 0.9\,T_1\,\eta$, $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(1, 0.04^2)$; the remaining channels (gate fidelities, readout error, error-per-Clifford, cross-resonance phase) are analogous bounded processes with small secular trends. A window is labelled out-of-specification when $T_1 < 50~\mu\text{s}$ or single-qubit fidelity $< 0.998$. $Q=5$ qubits, $N=200$ half-hour steps (100 hours), seven channels.

Leak-free windowing

Sliding windows, chronological splits

Per-qubit series are segmented into sliding windows of length $L=32$ predicting an $H=8$-step $T_1$ horizon, pooled across qubits: input $X_{\text{seq}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times 32\times 7}$, target $y_{\text{seq}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times 8}$, drift label $\ell\in\{0,1\}^{N}$. Leakage — a training window overlapping the future of a test window — is prevented by chronological splitting (never shuffling across the time axis): a $70/15/15$ split for the sequence-model benchmark, a per-qubit $80/20$ split for the forecasting baselines, and a randomised $70/30$ split for the unsupervised detector (safe only because it is fit on nominal windows alone). The scaler is fit on training data only and applied forward.

Ridge closed form

Multivariate ridge forecaster

With flattened windows as rows of $X$ and 8-step horizons as rows of $Y$, ridge (Tikhonov) regression solves

$$ \min_{W}\ \lVert XW - Y\rVert_2^2 + \alpha\lVert W\rVert_2^2 $$

with the unique closed form ($\alpha = 5$)

$$ W^\star = (X^\top X + \alpha I)^{-1} X^\top Y. $$

The $\alpha I$ term keeps $X^\top X + \alpha I$ invertible despite correlated channels and trades bias for variance. Baselines: persistence (carry last $T_1$), climatology (training-mean horizon), AR-ridge (the 32-step $T_1$ history only).

Horizon-skill theorem

Why skill widens with horizon

For a weakly stationary, zero-mean process with variance $\gamma_0$ and autocorrelation $\rho(h)$, persistence has risk $R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)=2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$ while the optimal single-lag predictor $\rho(h)\,x_t$ has risk $R_\star(h)=\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$, so the relative skill is

$$ S(h) = 1 - \frac{R_\star(h)}{R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)} = \frac{1-\rho(h)}{2}. $$

As $\rho(h)$ decays, $S(h)$ rises monotonically toward $\tfrac12$ (for an AR(1) process $\rho(h)=\phi^{h}$). This is a lower bound using only the last sample; the multivariate ridge exceeds it by exploiting the full 32-step, 7-channel history — which is why measured skill reaches 72% at horizon 8.

PCA reconstruction detector

Drift as off-manifold residual energy

Fit the top-$k$ right singular vectors $V_k$ of the centred nominal matrix $X$ and project a test window with $P_k = V_kV_k^\top$. The drift score is the off-subspace residual energy

$$ s_k(x) = \lVert x - xP_k\rVert_2^2. $$

Empirical optimality. Among all rank-$k$ orthogonal projectors, $P_k$ minimises $m^{-1}\lVert X - XP\rVert_F^2$ (Rayleigh–Ritz; unique under a strict spectral gap), so $s_k$ is the smallest residual any rank-$k$ linear monitor fitted on nominal data can give. When $k = d$, $P_k = I$, the score is constant and detection collapses to chance — a theorem, not a tuning artefact.

ROC-AUC (Mann–Whitney)

The scale-free yardstick for detection

For independent drift $X^{+}$ and nominal $X^{-}$ windows, the ROC area equals the probability a random positive outranks a random negative (ties at one half):

$$ \mathrm{AUC} = \Pr[s(X^{+}) > s(X^{-})] + \tfrac12\Pr[s(X^{+}) = s(X^{-})]. $$

AUC is therefore invariant under any strictly increasing reparametrisation of $s$ — in particular per-feature affine rescaling — which is why it, not MAE/RMSE, is the basis for the cross-domain comparison across datasets on wildly different scales. A constant score gives AUC exactly $\tfrac12$, the over-complete collapse mechanism.

Experimental Design & Statistics

How the numbers were produced

The benchmark deliberately separates quantities with replication from single-run point estimates, and reports the metric matched to each monitoring objective (horizon-stable MAE for forecasting, threshold-free ROC-AUC for detection).

Replication & uncertainty

What ± means here

Forecasting metrics are aggregated over n = 5 telemetry-generator seeds; the unsupervised reconstruction detector over n = 8 randomised splits. Reported uncertainties are mean ± standard deviation across those seeds/splits. The deep sequence-model metrics (thermal and cross-domain tables) are single deterministic, seeded notebook runs reported as point estimates — stated plainly, not dressed up as confidence intervals.

Leakage control

Train/test windowing

Chronological splits keep future intervals out of training: $70/15/15$ for the sequence-model benchmark, per-qubit $80/20$ for forecasting baselines, randomised $70/30$ for the detector (fit on nominal windows only). Scalers are fit on training data and applied forward. Window length 32, horizon 8, pooled across the five qubits.

Model families compared

Four sequence models + baselines

Single-layer Elman RNN, two-layer LSTM, two-layer GRU, and an encoder-only Transformer (pre-LN, $d_\text{model}=128$, 4 heads, 3 layers; sinusoidal positional encoding). Each emits an 8-step forecast and a drift logit, trained with AdamW (lr $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch 64, 30 epochs) under a cosine schedule, minimising $\mathrm{MSE}(\hat y, y) + \mathrm{BCE}(\sigma(z), \ell)$. Forecasting baselines: persistence, climatology, AR-ridge, multivariate ridge.

ROC-AUC methodology

Threshold-free ranking

Detection and anomaly ranking are scored by F1 at the default threshold and threshold-free ROC-AUC (the Mann–Whitney $U$). We report both because they disagree systematically: a model can rank intervals well (high AUC) yet detect nothing at a fixed operating point (zero F1). Cross-domain evaluation uses three Numenta (NAB) datasets as analogues for periodic, failure-bearing and irregular regimes; AUC's scale-invariance makes it the basis for that comparison.

Unsupervised drift detector

Low rank separates drift

The rank-3 reconstruction detector separates regimes with ROC-AUC 0.72 on a held-out split (0.70 ± 0.05 over 8 randomised splits). Sweeping the bottleneck rank shows detection is strongest at very low rank (0.87 ± 0.02 at rank 1) and collapses toward chance as the code becomes over-complete — because a high-capacity reconstruction memorises drifted windows as faithfully as nominal ones.

Drift lives off a low-rank nominal manifold; the rank is the false-alarm dial.
Key statistical comparison

F1 and ROC-AUC disagree by design

A model can rank intervals well (high ROC-AUC) yet detect nothing at a fixed threshold (zero F1) — visible in the thermal table, where only the GRU clears both bars. We therefore report both, and treat threshold selection as part of the monitoring problem rather than an afterthought.

Conclusion & Impact

Match the architecture to the monitoring objective

The work reframes quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as a tractable, decision-oriented ML problem and delivers an actionable rule: deploy the GRU as a default, parameter-efficient generalist and incident detector, and reserve the Transformer for signals known to be periodic. The recurring failure of strong models outside their niche — and the quiet robustness of the GRU — is a caution against benchmarking by a single headline metric. The low-rank manifold result connects the empirical detector to a geometric picture of drift and gives operators one interpretable dial.

Honest limitations (from the paper)

  • The telemetry generator is physically motivated but a model, not measured device data; cross-domain validation uses standard NAB anomaly-detection benchmarks as analogues, not archival quantum-calibration logs.
  • Absolute detection scores are modest, as expected for an honest, untuned benchmark on hard signals — no state-of-the-art claim is made.
  • The deep sequence-model metrics are single seeded runs (point estimates), not multi-seed confidence intervals.

Outlook

  • Validation on archival calibration records from operating processors.
  • Uncertainty-aware forecasting via Monte-Carlo dropout or deep ensembles.
  • Coupling the forecaster to a recalibration policy so predicted drift triggers action under an explicit cost model.

Because the pipeline is open, hardware-agnostic and fast to reproduce, it is positioned to serve as a common substrate for that work — a leakage-controlled, CPU-reproducible benchmark for quantum-processor reliability.
