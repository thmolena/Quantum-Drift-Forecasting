When Does Architecture Matter? Benchmarking Adaptive Sequence Models for Operational Drift Detection and Anomaly Localization

A reproducible three-experiment benchmark studying how architectural inductive bias shapes the forecast–detection trade-off across real operational time series from the Numenta Anomaly Benchmark.

Each experiment is structured as a joint task — multi-step forecasting paired with incident localization — so that accuracy and detection sensitivity are assessed simultaneously. The benchmark's central finding is a negative but operationally important result: no single architecture dominates all three objectives across all three regimes. GRU leads on low-error aggregation, LSTM leads on mean incident-F1, and the Transformer is most convincing as a calibration and ranking result.

Abstract

We present a reproducible benchmark studying how architectural inductive bias shapes the forecast–detection trade-off on real operational time series. Three self-contained experiments systematically evaluate vanilla recurrence, gated recurrent units (GRU), long short-term memory networks (LSTM), and Transformer-based self-attention across three distinct real-world temporal regimes drawn from the Numenta Anomaly Benchmark (NAB): equipment thermal failure, cloud CPU utilization, and urban demand. Each experiment jointly assesses multi-step forecast accuracy and incident-localization sensitivity so that no objective is treated in isolation. All results are produced by fully executed Jupyter notebooks with embedded figures and can be reproduced end-to-end on commodity CPU hardware.

Key Contributions

Three experimental contributions, each tied to a specific notebook, model family, and objective.

01

Experiment 1 — Adaptive Gating Improves Incident-Aware Forecasting

On real machine-temperature failure telemetry, GRU reduces forecast MAE by 15.60% relative to a vanilla recurrent baseline (final MAE 51.79, ROC-AUC 0.718) while simultaneously concentrating residuals around the documented failure regime — a joint forecasting and detection gain visualized through uncertainty-aware forecast envelopes, regime-separated residual boxplots, and an accuracy–efficiency scatter plot.

02

Experiment 2 — Transformer Is Strongest as a Calibration Result

On periodic EC2 CPU utilization telemetry, the Transformer achieves MAE 0.0436 and ROC-AUC 0.7987. Calibration scatter plots and threshold-sensitivity curves show that its anomaly score distribution provides a more informative ranking than any fixed-threshold F1 headline, making it the strongest detection model when the operating point can be tuned.

03

Experiment 3 — Model Selection Is Metric-Dependent

A cross-domain evaluation across all three NAB datasets shows that GRU leads on mean MAE (1337.33) and mean ROC-AUC (0.6603), while LSTM leads on mean incident-F1 (0.1057). No architecture dominates all objectives simultaneously. A cross-dataset performance heatmap and aggregate distribution charts deliver this conclusion visually.

Experiment 1 — Adaptive Gated Recurrent Forecasting on Thermal Failure Telemetry

Fig 1.1 — Drift Trajectory Overview

A multi-trace time-series of simulated coherence-time decay, used to illustrate the target forecasting structure before the real-data experiments. Each trajectory represents a distinct noise-level and drift-rate configuration, providing intuition for the signal geometry studied in Experiment 1.

A heatmap of pairwise Pearson correlations among the rolling-statistic features (mean, standard deviation, first difference) supplied to all recurrent models. Compact positive structure among the lag features confirms that the covariates carry complementary rather than redundant temporal information.

A two-panel time-series figure. The upper panel plots the raw machine-temperature signal with the documented failure interval shaded directly on the timeline. The lower panel overlays rolling mean, rolling standard deviation, and first-difference features, making local trend escalation and volatility change visible as the incident window approaches. Generated in Experiment 1, Figure 1.

Fig 1.4 — Optimization Dynamics and Headline Metrics

A four-subplot training summary: per-epoch training loss, per-epoch validation loss, a comparative MAE bar chart, and a comparative incident-F1 bar chart across VanillaRNN, LSTM, and GRU. Demonstrates that adaptive gating produces measurably lower error without requiring longer or more expensive optimization. Generated in Experiment 1, Figure 2.

Fig 1.5 — Uncertainty-Aware Forecast and Incident-Aligned Residuals

An MC-Dropout uncertainty envelope forecast (with conformal bound) for the best recurrent model, followed by a residual-intensity view aligned with binary incident labels. The key diagnostic question: does forecast difficulty concentrate near the failure window rather than remaining uniformly distributed across the test split? Generated in Experiment 1, Figure 3.

Fig 1.6 — Accuracy–Efficiency Frontier and Regime Separation

Four diagnostic subplots: (i) a relative MAE-reduction bar chart versus the vanilla baseline, (ii) a parameter-count × incident-F1 scatter colored by MAE exposing the accuracy–efficiency trade-off, (iii) a boxplot comparing residual distributions between nominal and incident windows, and (iv) a first-step forecast calibration scatter. The strongest evidence panel for recurrent model selection. Generated in Experiment 1, Figure 4.

Experiment 2 — Transformer Calibration on Cloud Telemetry

Fig 2.1 — EC2 Signal Structure and Anomaly Context

A multi-panel view of the raw EC2 CPU utilization signal with periodic structure annotated and documented anomaly intervals shaded. Temporal covariates (rolling statistics, differenced series) are overlaid to motivate the use of long-context self-attention on a periodically structured operational workload. Generated in Experiment 2, Figure 1.

Fig 2.2 — Transformer Training Dynamics

Per-epoch training and validation loss curves for the Transformer forecaster, demonstrating stable convergence under CPU-feasible training settings. Combined with the recurrent training curves from Experiment 1, these curves support a fair comparison of optimization cost across architectures. Generated in Experiment 2, Figure 2.

Fig 2.3 — Uncertainty-Aware Forecast and Reconstruction Anomaly Scores

A paired figure showing the Transformer's multi-step forecast with uncertainty bands alongside reconstruction-error-based anomaly scores aligned with documented anomaly intervals. This is the primary evidence that long-context representation improves incident concentration on periodic cloud telemetry. Generated in Experiment 2, Figure 3.

Fig 2.4 — Calibration Scatter, ROC Curve, and Threshold-Sensitivity Analysis

A multi-panel calibration analysis: (i) a forecast-versus-observed scatter diagnosing prediction alignment, (ii) a ROC curve summarizing anomaly ranking quality across all operating thresholds (AUC = 0.7987), and (iii) a threshold-sensitivity plot showing how F1 and anomaly-count co-vary across the score range. This suite motivates why ranking is a more informative summary than any single threshold F1. Generated in Experiment 2, Figure 4.

Experiment 3 — Cross-Domain Benchmark Comparison

Fig 3.1 — Multi-Dataset Signal Comparison

A three-panel time-series overview of the raw signals across machine temperature, EC2 CPU utilization, and NYC taxi demand, with documented anomaly intervals shaded in each panel. This figure makes regime differences — periodicity strength, background volatility, anomaly sparsity — visible before any architecture comparison is introduced. Generated in Experiment 3, Figure 1.

Fig 3.2 — Cross-Dataset Performance Heatmap

A metric-by-model-by-dataset heatmap compressing all cross-domain results into a single view. This is the central diagnostic figure of the benchmark: it shows which models rank first on MAE, F1, and ROC-AUC within each dataset and reveals whether those rankings are consistent across domains. Generated in Experiment 3, Figure 2.

Fig 3.3 — Aggregate Performance Distributions

Box plots of per-dataset metric distributions across all model families, revealing whether any architecture achieves consistently low spread across all three temporal regimes. Wide spread on a given metric indicates objective-dependent sensitivity to dataset characteristics. Generated in Experiment 3, Figure 3.

Fig 3.4 — Metric-Dependent Model Preference

A structured comparison of mean MAE, mean F1, and mean ROC-AUC across all model families, together with within-dataset rank annotations. The benchmark's main conclusion is delivered here: GRU leads on mean MAE (1337.33) and mean ROC-AUC (0.66), LSTM leads on mean F1 (0.11), and no architecture dominates all three objectives simultaneously. Generated in Experiment 3, Figure 4.

Results Summary

Headline metrics across all three experiments. The benchmark's primary claim — that model preference is metric-dependent rather than architecturally universal — is supported by the disagreement between MAE-optimal and F1-optimal model rankings in Experiment 3.

RNN

VanillaRNN — Baseline

Minimal recurrent baseline. Establishes the lower bound from which gated models demonstrate measurable gains. Used in Experiment 1.

LSTM

LSTMForecaster — Best Mean F1

Higher-capacity gated architecture. Achieves best mean incident-F1 (0.1057) across all three datasets in Experiment 3, making it the preferred model when detection sensitivity is the deployment objective.

GRU

GRUForecaster — Best Mean MAE and ROC-AUC

Lighter gated alternative. Achieves the best mean MAE (1337.33) and best mean ROC-AUC (0.6603) across all three datasets in Experiment 3, and produces the strongest single-dataset result in Experiment 1 with a 15.60% MAE reduction over VanillaRNN.

Transformer

TransformerForecaster — Best Calibration

Long-context self-attention model. Achieves the highest ROC-AUC (0.7987) on periodic EC2 telemetry in Experiment 2, and is most convincing as a calibration and score-ranking result rather than a single-threshold detection claim.

Reproducibility

All experiments run end-to-end on commodity CPU hardware. Stochastic operations are seeded at the entry point of each notebook. The commands below re-execute all three notebooks and regenerate the full HTML report suite including all embedded figures. 

conda activate qaoa
pip install -r requirements.txt

# Re-execute all three experiments
jupyter nbconvert --to notebook --execute --inplace notebooks/rnn_drift_forecast.ipynb
jupyter nbconvert --to notebook --execute --inplace notebooks/transformer_calibration.ipynb
jupyter nbconvert --to notebook --execute --inplace notebooks/quantum_drift_combined.ipynb

# Export HTML reports with all embedded figures
jupyter nbconvert --to html --output-dir website/notebooks_html \
  notebooks/rnn_drift_forecast.ipynb \
  notebooks/transformer_calibration.ipynb \
  notebooks/quantum_drift_combined.ipynb

# Optional: start the local inference API for the interactive demo
python -m src.server --port 5000
