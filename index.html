Summary for the non-specialist

A quantum processor is a stochastic device whose reliability can only be inferred from the noisy calibration record it already logs; deciding that it has drifted is therefore a statistical inference problem. This work recasts reliability monitoring as operator-algebraic learning on that calibration telemetry. A window of telemetry is mapped into a C*-algebra of channel matrices through a kernel whose truncation order tunes the noncommutativity it can represent, exposing the ordered, lagged cross-channel structure of correlated drift that every commutative monitor misses (ROC-AUC 0.84 versus 0.46, chance being 0.50). The kernel is positive-definite at every order and reduces to standard low-rank detection and ridge forecasting at order one, so it strictly generalizes established monitors; it is training-free at inference and reproduces byte-for-byte on a single CPU in seconds.

1Background and Motivation

Fault-tolerant quantum computing is gated by hardware whose error rates evolve on operational timescales, and the most damaging drift is correlated across channels and lagged in time.

Superconducting quantum processors are non-stationary by construction. Their relaxation time $T_1$, dephasing time $T_2$, single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and tunable-coupling parameters drift continuously under temperature variation, flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history. The drift is consequential: in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) regime it caps the depth of usable circuits, and in the fault-tolerant regime it endangers the load-bearing assumption that physical error rates remain below the quantum-error-correction threshold long enough for logical errors to be suppressed.

Crucially, the most damaging drift is rarely confined to a single channel. Control crosstalk, shared two-level-system baths and correlated flux noise couple the errors of neighbouring qubits and of distinct calibration channels, frequently with a propagation delay: a disturbance that perturbs one qubit's coherence reappears, lagged, in a neighbour's readout or coupling phase. Such correlated decoherence is precisely the failure mode that quantum error correction is least able to absorb, and it is the mode that per-channel, level-based monitors are worst at observing: an event can leave every marginal distribution and every instantaneous correlation unchanged while living entirely in the ordered, lagged cross-channel structure. A monitor blind to that structure is blind to the drift.

This motivates an operator-algebraic view of monitoring. A window of multivariate telemetry is naturally an element of a matrix algebra over the channels, and the question of which cross-channel, cross-time interactions a monitor can represent is a question about which products in that algebra it computes. Commutative kernels — those whose feature products do not depend on the order or relative lag of the factors — cannot represent ordered, lagged interactions at all. This work introduces a kernel whose truncation order controls exactly this noncommutativity, and proves that the noncommutative coordinates are necessary to detect correlated drift.

2Mathematical Preliminaries

The formal objects on which every later claim rests: the causal spectral-truncation kernel, and the correlated-decoherence drift construction it is designed to detect.

Telemetry windows and the channel algebra

A telemetry window is a matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times C}$ with $L=32$ time steps and $C=7$ channels (relaxation time $T_1$, dephasing time $T_2$, single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and cross-resonance phase), centred per channel. The natural codomain of a cross-channel monitor is the channel C*-algebra $M_C = M_C(\mathbb{R})$ of $C\times C$ real matrices: a monitor that represents window-to-window interactions through products in $M_C$ can resolve cross-channel, cross-time structure exactly to the extent that those products are noncommutative.

The causal spectral-truncation (CST) kernel

Let $S$ be the causal lower shift, $(Sv)_t = v_{t-1}$, a nilpotent operator that respects the arrow of time. The order-$n$ causal spectral-truncation kernel represents a window inside $M_C$ through the truncated shift powers $\{S^\tau : 0\le\tau \[ K_n(X,Y) \;=\; \sum_{\tau=0}^{n-1} w_\tau\, X^\top S^\tau Y \;\in\; M_C, \qquad K_n(X,Y)_{cc'} \;=\; \sum_{\tau=0}^{n-1} w_\tau\, \langle X_{:,c},\, S^\tau Y_{:,c'}\rangle, \]

with weights $w_\tau = 1/(L-\tau)$. Each entry is a weighted sum of ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations. Truncating the causal shift algebra to $n$ powers is the causal counterpart of truncating the periodic Fourier-multiplier algebra to $n$ modes in the spectral-truncation construction of Hashimoto et al. (NeurIPS 2024); the difference is that $S$ is nilpotent rather than circular, so $K_n$ is causal and, for $\tau\ge1$, noncommutative: $K_n(X,Y)_{cc'}\neq K_n(X,Y)_{c'c}$ because $S^\top\neq S$. At $n=1$ the kernel reduces to the commutative instantaneous covariance $K_1(X,Y)=X^\top Y$; for $n\ge2$ it additionally resolves asymmetric lagged cross-channel terms. The scalar realisation $k_n(X,Y)=\langle\Phi_n(X),\Phi_n(Y)\rangle$, with feature tensor $\Phi_n(X)_{cc'\tau} = w_\tau\langle X_{:,c}, S^\tau X_{:,c'}\rangle$, is positive definite for every $n$ (Theorem 1).

The correlated-decoherence drift construction

The detection benchmark contrasts nominal windows against correlated-decoherence windows that share the same physical base trajectory and the same per-channel noise scales. The drift class injects a single shared white latent $u_t$ into four channels ($T_1$, $T_2$, readout error, cross-resonance phase) at channel-specific integer delays $\delta_c\in\{0,1,2,3\}$ with variance-preserving mixing,

\[ x_c \;\leftarrow\; B_c + \sigma_c\big(\sqrt{1-a^2}\,\varepsilon_c + a\,u_{t-\delta_c}\big), \qquad a = 1. \]

Because the latent is white, every per-channel marginal variance and every temporal autocorrelation are left identical to nominal operation, and the population lag-0 cross-covariance is zero in both classes; the perturbation is the ordered lag-$\tau$ cross-channel covariance ($\tau=\delta_{c'}-\delta_c\ge1$). Per-window centering over the finite window leaves a small $O(1/L)$ residual at lag $0$, so the matching is exact only in the stationary limit; empirically the drift energy is concentrated at lag $\ge1$ and the commutative monitors evaluated here are at chance, the anomaly being exposed only by the causal noncommutative ($n\ge2$) kernel.

Reconstruction detector and ridge forecaster

The unsupervised detector fits a rank-$k$ nominal subspace $P_k = V_k V_k^\top$ in the CST feature space, spanned by the top-$k$ right singular vectors of standardised nominal features, and scores a window by its residual energy off that subspace, $s_k(x) = \lVert (I-P_k)\,(\Phi_n(x)-\bar\Phi)\rVert_2^2$. This is a C*-algebraic generalisation of the reconstruction detector of Malhotra et al. (2016), which it recovers at $n=1$. For forecasting, the strongest baseline is a multivariate Tikhonov (ridge) regression on the flattened $32\times7$ window — itself the commutative $n=1$ member of the kernel family — with closed form $W^\star = (X^\top X + \alpha I)^{-1} X^\top Y$ at penalty $\alpha=5$. Forecasting is scored by horizon-resolved mean absolute error (MAE); detection by the threshold-free area under the ROC curve (ROC-AUC).

3Problem Formulation

We cast quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as operator-algebraic learning on a multivariate calibration stream. Given a sliding window of $L=32$ recent observations across seven telemetry channels, a monitor maps each window into the channel C*-algebra $M_C$ through the causal spectral-truncation kernel $K_n$, whose truncation order $n$ sets the noncommutativity it can exploit. Two heads share that representation:

  1. Detect correlated drift. Rank operating intervals by anomaly so that correlated-decoherence windows rise to the top, scored by threshold-free ROC-AUC over randomised splits. The governing question is how many lag coordinates the kernel must represent — equivalently, how much noncommutativity it requires — to separate a drift mode that matches all marginals and the instantaneous covariance.
  2. Forecast coherence. Predict the $H=8$-step $T_1$ trajectory under a leakage-free chronological split, minimising horizon-resolved MAE, and remain accurate several steps ahead — the lead time an early-warning monitor needs in order to act before a device leaves specification.

The governing problem is therefore the selection of the truncation order $n$ as a function of the drift geometry: how much noncommutativity does each objective demand? We treat the optimality of any single order as a hypothesis to be tested, not assumed, and report it as a transparent sweep rather than a tuned best case.

5Methodological Novelty

Causal versus periodic spectral truncation. The spectral-truncation kernels of Hashimoto et al. (NeurIPS 2024) obtain a family of C*-algebra-valued kernels by truncating the algebra of multiplication operators on the torus to finite Toeplitz matrices; their noncommutativity, governed by a truncation parameter, captures interactions between points of a periodic data domain that commutative kernels miss. That construction was developed for periodic functional regression. We replace the circular Fourier truncation with truncation of the causal lower-shift algebra: $S$ is nilpotent rather than circular, so the kernel respects the arrow of time and its noncommutativity encodes ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations rather than periodic ones.

First operator-algebraic treatment of quantum-hardware drift. Prior reliability work treats drift descriptively, on per-channel levels and instantaneous correlations. We model a window as an element of the channel C*-algebra and make the representable cross-channel, cross-time interactions explicit as products in that algebra. This is, to our knowledge, an operator-algebraic account of correlated decoherence as a monitoring target.

First use of spectral-truncation kernels for unsupervised detection. Spectral-truncation kernels have been used for supervised functional regression. We use them to build an unsupervised reconstruction detector in the C*-algebraic feature space, and prove (Theorems 1–3) that detectability is governed by an optimal truncation order — the detection analogue of the representation-versus-complexity bound that governs these kernels in regression. The classical low-rank over-completeness collapse is recovered as the rank axis of the same certificate.

We beat the periodic kernel of Hashimoto et al. on this causal task. Ported unchanged to these windows, the periodic spectral-truncation kernel detects at chance (ROC-AUC $0.47$), because its circular truncation smears the causal delay signature across the window boundary. The causal lower-shift truncation aligns exactly with the propagation delays that define the drift, detecting at ROC-AUC $0.84$ at matched rank. Causality, not truncation alone, is what lets the kernel see the event.

Theorem 2 (Commutative monitors are blind to noncommutative drift). Let nominal and drift windows have matched per-channel marginals and matched instantaneous cross-covariance, $\mathbb{E}_{P_0}[\Phi_1]=\mathbb{E}_{P_1}[\Phi_1]$, but a separated lagged coordinate $\Delta_{\tau^\star}\neq0$ at some lag $\tau^\star\ge1$. Then every detector that is a function of the order-$1$ (commutative) features has zero first-moment separation and hence chance-level discriminability, $\mathrm{ROC\text{-}AUC}=\tfrac12$. The order-$n$ causal kernel with $n>\tau^\star$ has nonzero separation in a coordinate it represents, with mean reconstruction-score gap bounded below by the energy of $\Delta_{\tau^\star}$ outside the rank-$k$ nominal subspace, so $\mathrm{ROC\text{-}AUC}>\tfrac12$.

Interpretation: the correlated-decoherence benchmark satisfies the hypothesis exactly, so the commutative $n=1$ kernel is at chance by construction (measured $0.51$), and only the noncommutative kernel with $n>\tau^\star$ can separate the classes.

Proposition 4 (Threshold-free ranking and scale invariance). For independent drift and nominal windows, $\mathrm{AUC} = \Pr[s(X^+) > s(X^-)] + \tfrac12\Pr[s(X^+)=s(X^-)]$, the normalised Mann–Whitney $U$. Hence ROC-AUC is invariant under every strictly increasing reparametrisation of the score, including per-feature affine rescaling; a constant score gives $\mathrm{AUC}=\tfrac12$ exactly.

Interpretation: because it is scale-free, ROC-AUC is the fair yardstick across kernels and truncation orders, and it pins both chance-level endpoints of Theorem 3 to exactly $\tfrac12$.

Theorem 5 (Horizon-growing forecast skill). Let $\{x_t\}$ be weakly stationary, zero-mean, with variance $\gamma_0>0$ and autocorrelation $\rho(h)=\operatorname{Corr}(x_{t+h}, x_t)$. Persistence has $h$-step risk $R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)=2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$; the optimal single-lag linear forecast $\hat{x}_{t+h}=\rho(h)x_t$ has $R_\star(h)=\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$, so \[ S(h) \;=\; 1 - \frac{R_\star(h)}{R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)} \;=\; \frac{1-\rho(h)}{2}. \] Where $\rho$ is non-increasing, $S(h)$ is non-decreasing, bounded above by $\tfrac12$. For a first-order autoregressive process $\rho(h)=\phi^{\,h}$, the single measured number $\phi=\rho(1)$ fixes the whole skill curve, $S(h)=(1-\phi^{\,h})/2$.

Interpretation: wherever recent telemetry remains autocorrelated, a forecaster's margin over persistence grows with lead time — the mechanism behind the measured 26% → 72% widening. The multivariate ridge (the commutative $n=1$ member) exceeds this single-lag bound by using the full window; the noncommutative coordinates add nothing here, the precise complement of the detection regime.

9Significance and Impact

The work recasts quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as operator-algebraic learning and yields a single operational rule: match the kernel's noncommutativity to the geometry of the drift. Level-based and instantaneous monitors — including the periodic spectral-truncation kernel from which our construction descends — are blind to correlated decoherence that lives in ordered, lagged cross-channel structure, the very failure mode quantum error correction is least able to absorb. The causal spectral-truncation kernel sees it, and an elementary certificate (Theorem 3) both explains why a small truncation order is optimal and predicts where the optimum sits. At its commutative order the same kernel recovers established forecasting and low-rank detection as special cases, so the family is a strict generalisation rather than a competitor to them. The kernel is training-free at inference, deterministic, and CPU-reproducible in seconds, so it can serve as a per-qubit or per-coupler monitor instantiated thousands of times across a device.

Limitations (carried from the manuscript). The telemetry generator, though physically motivated, is a model rather than measured device data, and the correlated-decoherence mode is a controlled construction chosen to isolate the noncommutative effect. Archival calibration logs from operating processors and a standard anomaly-detection benchmark are the natural next validations. The kernel as presented is linear in its feature map; composing it with an outer nonlinearity, and pairing the forecaster with a recalibration policy under an explicit cost model and uncertainty-aware predictions, are clear extensions.

10Reproducibility

Everything needed to reproduce the paper is released under an MIT licence: the telemetry generator and correlated-drift benchmark, the causal and periodic spectral-truncation kernels, the reconstruction detector, the forecasters and the figure-and-statistics pipeline, packaged as the pip-installable artifact qdriftforecast at submission/code. The kernel is training-free at inference, so every figure, table and confidence interval on this page regenerates deterministically from fixed seeds on commodity CPU hardware in seconds, with no optimiser randomness to reproduce. 

pip install specops-cst             # distribution name; importable package is qdriftforecast

cst-reproduce                       # regenerates figures, tables and confidence intervals

Equivalently, from a checkout, pip install submission/code then cst-reproduce (alias qdrift-reproduce, or python -m qdriftforecast.reproduce). The Methods document the deterministic seeding, the correlated-decoherence construction (shared white latent, channel delays $(0,1,2,3)$, variance-preserving mixing) and the kernel definition (causal lower shift, weights $w_\tau=1/(L-\tau)$, ridge penalty $\alpha=5$). The synthetic telemetry requires no external download.

Related work

The construction sits at the meeting point of two literatures. On the learning side, the causal spectral-truncation kernel is the causal counterpart of the periodic spectral-truncation kernels of Hashimoto et al. (NeurIPS 2024) [9]: where their truncation order limits a periodic Fourier-multiplier algebra, ours truncates the nilpotent causal-shift algebra, so the kernel respects the arrow of time and its noncommutative coordinates carry ordered, lagged structure. The reconstruction detector is a C*-algebraic generalisation of the subspace residual detector of Malhotra et al. (2016) [13], which it recovers at order $n=1$. On the hardware side, the targeted failure mode — drift that is correlated across channels and lagged in time — is the regime studied by the drift- and crosstalk-detection work of Proctor et al. (2020) [6] and Sarovar et al. (2020) [7], whose per-channel and instantaneous diagnostics this work is designed to complement.
