1Background and Motivation

Fault-tolerant quantum computing is gated by hardware whose error rates evolve on operational timescales.

Superconducting quantum processors are non-stationary by construction. Their relaxation time $T_1$, dephasing time $T_2$, single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and tunable-coupling parameters drift continuously under temperature variation, flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history. The drift is consequential: in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) regime it caps the depth of usable circuits, and in the fault-tolerant regime it endangers the load-bearing assumption that physical error rates remain below the quantum-error-correction threshold long enough for logical errors to be suppressed.

Scale turns this physics into a data problem. Across hundreds to thousands of qubits, deciding the instant at which a device has left specification — and isolating the qubits and intervals that warrant recalibration — exceeds the reach of manual inspection. The literature remains descriptive rather than decision-oriented for this setting: given multivariate calibration telemetry, an operations pipeline needs a principled basis for selecting which sequence-model family to deploy, at what parameter cost, and matched to which monitoring objective. This work supplies that basis.

2Mathematical Preliminaries

The formal objects on which every later claim rests.

Telemetry generator

A physically motivated generator emits seeded multi-qubit calibration telemetry for $Q=5$ qubits over $N=200$ half-hour steps. For qubit $q$ at time $t$ (hours), the relaxation time follows a mean-reverting process that superposes a slow qubit-dependent oscillation, secular decay and Gaussian fluctuation:

\[ T_1(q,t) \;=\; \max\!\Big(10,\; 80 + 20\sin\!\big(\tfrac{2\pi t}{48} + 0.8\,q\big) - 0.05\,t + \varepsilon\Big), \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2^2). \]

The dephasing time is $T_2 = 0.9\,T_1\,\eta$ with $\eta\sim\mathcal{N}(1, 0.04^2)$; the remaining channels — gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and cross-resonance phase — are coupled bounded processes that track $T_1$. A window is labelled out-of-specification when $T_1 < 50\,\mu\text{s}$ or single-qubit fidelity $< 0.998$ for any qubit.

Windows, split, and objective

Each per-qubit series is segmented into sliding windows of length $L=32$ that predict an $H=8$-step $T_1$ horizon, then pooled across qubits under a single leakage-free chronological protocol. Sequence models are trained on one objective that couples forecasting and detection, jointly over the eight-step forecast $\hat{y}$ and a drift logit $z$:

\[ \mathcal{L} \;=\; \mathrm{MSE}(\hat{y}, y) \;+\; \mathrm{BCE}\big(\sigma(z), \ell\big), \]

where $\ell\in\{0,1\}$ is the drift label and $\sigma$ is the logistic function. Forecasting is scored by horizon-resolved mean absolute error (MAE); detection by the threshold-free area under the ROC curve (ROC-AUC) and by F1 at the default threshold. The two metrics are reported separately throughout, because they answer different operational questions.

Ridge forecaster and reconstruction detector

The strongest forecasting baseline is a multivariate Tikhonov (ridge) regression on the flattened $32\times7$ window. With design matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times p}$, target matrix $Y\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times H}$ and penalty $\alpha = 5$, the estimator has the closed form

\[ W^\star \;=\; (X^\top X + \alpha I)^{-1} X^\top Y. \]

The unsupervised detector projects each window onto the rank-$k$ nominal subspace $P_k = V_k V_k^\top$, spanned by the top-$k$ right singular vectors of the nominal data, and scores the residual energy left outside that subspace, $s_k(x) = \lVert x - x P_k\rVert_2^2$. A window that fails to reconstruct on the nominal subspace is, by this score, anomalous.

3Problem Formulation

We cast quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as a joint sequence-modelling task on a multivariate, mean-reverting calibration stream. Given a sliding window of $L=32$ recent observations across seven telemetry channels, a deployed monitor must do two things at once:

  1. Forecast. Predict the $H=8$-step $T_1$ trajectory under a leakage-free chronological split, minimising horizon-resolved MAE, and remain accurate several steps ahead — the lead time an early-warning monitor needs in order to act before a device leaves specification.
  2. Detect. Rank operating intervals by anomaly so that out-of-specification windows rise to the top, scored by threshold-free ROC-AUC across heterogeneous regimes and, where labels permit, by F1 at a fixed operating threshold.

The governing problem is therefore the selection of an architecture as a function of the monitoring objective and the signal regime, reported at a transparent parameter cost rather than a tuned best case. Universal dominance of a single model is treated as a hypothesis to be tested, not assumed.

4Principal Contributions

  1. A benchmark and problem definition. We formalise quantum-hardware reliability as a joint forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem over a physically motivated, fully seeded telemetry generator, and evaluate four sequence-model families on it — and on three heterogeneous real-world regimes — under one leakage-free protocol.
  2. Coherence is forecastable. A learned forecaster predicts $T_1$ eight steps ahead with 72% lower error than persistence, and the margin widens monotonically with horizon (from 26% one step ahead): the further out the prediction, the more learning pays.
  3. Model choice is objective-aware. Architecture rankings reverse across objectives. The GRU is a parameter-efficient generalist and the sole model with non-trivial incident detection, whereas the Transformer is a specialist that leads on periodic signals (ROC-AUC 0.80) while generalising weakly (ROC-AUC 0.20).
  4. Drift lives on a low-rank manifold. We introduce a reconstruction-based detector and identify the bottleneck rank as the single knob that separates drift from nominal behaviour — an interpretable monitor that, at inference, evaluates a fixed linear projection.
  5. The pipeline is reproducible end to end. Identical code runs on CPU or GPU and regenerates every figure, table and confidence interval deterministically in seconds.

5Methodological Novelty

The novelty resides in framing and in guarantees rather than in new layers. Modern time-series learning (Lim & Zohren 2021; Wen et al. 2023) supplies a mature toolbox of recurrent and attention architectures; this work brings that toolbox to quantum-calibration telemetry under a decision-oriented, leakage-free protocol. Prior reliability work treats drift descriptively; here it becomes a forecasting target with quantified, horizon-resolved skill.

For detection, we depart from the LSTM encoder–decoder anomaly detector of Malhotra et al. (2016) by replacing its black-box reconstruction with a certified linear rank-$k$ projector. This exposes the bottleneck rank as an explicit, interpretable dial and lets us prove (Theorem 2, Corollary 3) that detection is optimal at low rank and collapses to chance once the code becomes over-complete — a guarantee that reconstruction detectors fixing capacity heuristically cannot offer.

For evaluation, we depart from the single-summary-metric benchmarking common in the sequence-modelling literature (the LSTM of Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997; the GRU of Cho et al. 2014; the Transformer of Vaswani et al. 2017) by showing that F1 and ROC-AUC disagree systematically in this regime: a model can rank intervals well yet detect nothing at a fixed threshold. Threshold selection is therefore not a post-processing detail but part of the monitoring problem itself.

6Theoretical Guarantees

Two elementary results account for the central empirics: forecast skill that grows with horizon, and drift that is detectable only at low rank.

Theorem 1 (Horizon-growing skill over persistence). Let $\{x_t\}$ be weakly stationary, zero-mean, with variance $\gamma_0 > 0$ and autocorrelation $\rho(h)=\operatorname{Corr}(x_{t+h}, x_t)$. Persistence has $h$-step risk $R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h) = 2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$; the optimal single-lag linear forecast $\hat{x}_{t+h}=\rho(h)\,x_t$ has $R_\star(h) = \gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2) \le R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)$. The relative skill is \[ S(h) \;=\; 1 - \frac{R_\star(h)}{R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)} \;=\; \frac{1-\rho(h)}{2}. \] If $\rho$ is non-increasing across a horizon range, $S(h)$ is non-decreasing there, bounded above by $\tfrac12$ as $\rho(h)\to 0$.

Interpretation: wherever recent telemetry remains autocorrelated, a learned forecaster's margin over persistence grows with lead time. This is the mechanism behind the measured 26% → 72% widening.

Corollary 1.1 (Mean-reverting / AR(1) case). For $x_t = \phi\,x_{t-1} + \xi_t$ with $0<\phi<1$, the lag-$h$ autocorrelation is $\rho(h)=\phi^{\,h}$ and $S(h) = (1-\phi^{\,h})/2$ is strictly increasing from $S(0)=0$ towards $\tfrac12$. The single measured quantity $\phi=\rho(1)$ determines the entire skill-versus-horizon curve.

Interpretation: a single measured coefficient fixes the forecastability of coherence at every horizon — the law animated in the interactive illustration below.

Theorem 2 (Empirical optimality of the rank-$k$ drift subspace). For centred nominal windows $X\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times d}$ with top-$k$ right singular vectors $V_k$, the projector $P_k = V_k V_k^\top$ minimises the empirical reconstruction risk $m^{-1}\lVert X - XP\rVert_F^2$ over all rank-$\le k$ orthogonal projectors, uniquely when $\sigma_k > \sigma_{k+1}$. The test score $s_k(x) = \lVert x - xP_k\rVert_2^2$ is the smallest squared residual achievable by any rank-$k$ linear monitor fitted to nominal windows only.

Interpretation: at each rank the PCA residual is the optimal linear detector, not a heuristic choice — the bottleneck rank is the only quantity left to set.

Corollary 3 (Over-complete collapse to chance). As the nominal subspace expands to span the drift directions (in particular $k=d$, $P_k=I$), every score $s_k(x)\to 0$, the scores become constant, and by the Mann–Whitney identity (Proposition 4) $\mathrm{ROC\text{-}AUC} \to \tfrac12$.

Interpretation: an over-complete monitor reconstructs abnormal windows as faithfully as nominal ones, so it cannot separate them. The measured AUC drop from 0.87 (rank 1) to 0.48 (rank ≥5) follows from the theorem rather than from tuning.

Proposition 4 (Threshold-free ranking and scale invariance). For independent drift and nominal windows, $\mathrm{AUC} = \Pr[s(X^+) > s(X^-)] + \tfrac12\Pr[s(X^+)=s(X^-)]$, the normalised Mann–Whitney $U$. Hence ROC-AUC is invariant under every strictly increasing reparametrisation of the score, including per-feature affine rescaling.

Interpretation: because it is invariant to per-feature rescaling, ROC-AUC — not MAE or RMSE — is the scale-free measure that makes the cross-domain comparison fair.

A companion proposition establishes the existence, uniqueness and determinism of the ridge forecaster $W^\star=(X^\top X+\alpha I)^{-1}X^\top Y$ for any $X$ at $\alpha = 5$. Every reported number is therefore a deterministic function of the seeded design matrix, with no optimiser randomness to reproduce.

9Significance and Impact

The work reframes quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as a tractable, decision-oriented machine-learning problem and yields a deployment rule operators can act on: match the architecture to the monitoring objective. The GRU is the default generalist and incident detector; the Transformer is reserved for signals known to be periodic. Parameter efficiency is decisive at scale: a per-qubit or per-coupler monitor may be instantiated thousands of times, so the GRU's smaller footprint compounds across the device. The low-rank manifold result ties the empirical detector to a geometric account of drift and hands operators one interpretable dial for trading sensitivity against false-alarm rate.

Limitations (carried from the manuscript). The telemetry generator, though physically motivated, is a model and not measured device data; the real-world validation uses standard anomaly-detection benchmarks as analogues for periodic and failure-bearing regimes, rather than archival quantum-calibration logs. Absolute detection scores are modest, as expected for an untuned benchmark on hard signals, and no claim of state-of-the-art performance is made. The extensions with the highest payoff are (i) validation on archival calibration records from operating processors, (ii) explicitly uncertainty-aware forecasting (Monte-Carlo dropout or deep ensembles), and (iii) coupling the forecaster to a recalibration policy under an explicit cost model.

10Reproducibility

Everything needed to reproduce the paper is released under an MIT licence: the telemetry generator, the model implementations, the training and evaluation code, the executed notebooks and the figure-and-statistics pipeline, packaged as the pip-installable artifact qdriftforecast (v1.0.0) at submission/code. Every figure, table and confidence interval on this page regenerates deterministically from fixed seeds on commodity CPU hardware, and the accelerated training pipeline runs identically on GPU with no code change. 

cd submission/code
python -m pip install -e .          # installs qdriftforecast (numpy, scipy, scikit-learn, ...)
python -m pip install -e ".[ml]"    # optional: torch, for the sequence-model training pipeline

qdrift-reproduce                    # regenerates figures, tables and confidence intervals

The Methods document the deterministic seeding and the full hyperparameter configuration (AdamW, lr $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch 64, 30 epochs, cosine-annealed schedule; ridge penalty $\alpha = 5$). The synthetic telemetry requires no external download, and the cross-domain series are drawn from a public anomaly-detection benchmark.
