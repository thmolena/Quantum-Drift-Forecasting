% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Fault-tolerant quantum computing is gated by hardware that drifts: qubit
coherence times, gate fidelities and calibration parameters wander on timescales
of hours, eroding the error budgets that quantum error correction depends on. We
recast reliability monitoring as \emph{operator-algebraic} learning on
multivariate calibration telemetry and introduce the causal spectral-truncation
(CST) kernel --- a C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra-valued positive-definite
kernel whose truncation order $n$ controls the noncommutativity of the
cross-channel products it represents. It is the causal counterpart of the
spectral-truncation kernels recently developed for functional data on the
torus~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral}: in place of a periodic Fourier truncation we
truncate the \emph{causal shift} algebra, so the kernel respects the arrow of
time and its noncommutativity encodes ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations.
The central finding is a separation that only a noncommutative monitor can see.
We construct a physically motivated correlated-decoherence (crosstalk) drift mode
with matched per-channel marginals and, in the stationary limit, matched
instantaneous cross-covariance, so the drift signal is concentrated in the
ordered, lagged cross-channel structure. The commutative monitors we evaluate ---
raw-level, instantaneous-covariance and even the periodic spectral-truncation
kernel --- all sit at chance (ROC-AUC $\approx0.5$), whereas the causal
noncommutative kernel detects the drift at ROC-AUC~$0.90$ (and $0.84$ at matched
bottleneck rank), through a sharp optimal truncation order that we certify
theoretically as the detection analogue of the representation--complexity
trade-off. The same kernel forecasts coherence with
horizon-growing skill ($72\%$ over persistence eight steps ahead), a regime in
which noncommutativity is unnecessary --- yielding an operational rule: match the
kernel's noncommutativity to the geometry of the drift. The entire pipeline is
hardware-agnostic, installs with \texttt{pip}, and regenerates every figure and
table deterministically on commodity CPU hardware in seconds.
\end{abstract}

% ── Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Quantum processors are not static devices. The relaxation time \T, the dephasing
time \Ttwo, single- and two-qubit gate fidelities, readout-assignment error and
tunable-coupling parameters all evolve continuously as a function of temperature,
flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history
\citep{krantz2019guide,kjaergaard2020superconducting}. This drift is more than a
nuisance: in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) regime it limits the
depth of usable circuits \citep{preskill2018nisq}, and in the fault-tolerant
regime it threatens the very assumption that physical error rates remain below
threshold for long enough for error correction to suppress logical errors
\citep{google2023surfacecode,arute2019supremacy}. As processors scale to
hundreds and thousands of qubits, deciding \emph{when} a device has drifted out
of specification, and \emph{which} qubits or intervals warrant recalibration,
becomes a data problem at a scale that manual inspection cannot meet.

Crucially, the most damaging drift is rarely confined to one channel. Control
crosstalk, shared two-level-system baths and correlated flux noise couple the
errors of neighbouring qubits and of different calibration channels, often with a
\emph{propagation delay}: a disturbance that perturbs one qubit's coherence
reappears, lagged, in a neighbour's readout or coupling phase
\citep{krantz2019guide}. Such \emph{correlated decoherence} is precisely the
failure mode that quantum error correction is least able to absorb, yet it is the
mode that per-channel, level-based monitors are worst at seeing: an event can
leave every marginal distribution and every instantaneous correlation unchanged
while living entirely in the \emph{ordered, lagged} cross-channel structure. A
monitor blind to that structure is blind to the drift.

This motivates an operator-algebraic view. The temporal evolution of calibration
telemetry is a multivariate stochastic process; a window of it is naturally an
element of a matrix algebra over the channels, and the question of which
cross-channel, cross-time interactions a monitor can represent is a question
about which products in that algebra it computes --- the setting of operator- and
vector-valued kernel learning~\citep{micchelli2005vector}. Commutative kernels ---
those whose feature products do not depend on the order or relative lag of the
factors --- cannot represent ordered lagged interactions at all. The recent
spectral-truncation kernels of Hashimoto et~al.\ make this precise for functional
data: by truncating the algebra of multiplication operators on the torus to
finite Toeplitz matrices, they obtain a family of C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}%
-algebra-valued kernels whose noncommutativity, governed by a truncation
parameter, captures interactions between different points of the data domain that
commutative kernels miss~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral}. That construction was
developed for periodic functional regression; it has not been brought to bear on
time-ordered hardware telemetry, on causal monitoring, or on unsupervised
detection.

We close that gap. This reliability study is the sixth manuscript in an ordered
quantum-AI series that treats resource accounting and CPU-reproducible evidence
as design constraints rather than afterthoughts. Earlier papers study query
allocation for QAOA, topology-conditioned warm starts, commutator-graph term
ordering, learned Trotter residuals, and CPTP-constrained open-system
identification~\citep{huynh2026uqqaoa,huynh2026topoqaoa,huynh2026topoham,huynh2026liergtc,huynh2026cptppinn};
the Hamiltonian-simulation work of Nguyen and Jing provides complementary
fixed-depth and representation-theoretic
context~\citep{nguyen2025zassenhaus,jing2026complexity}. Here the object of study
is time-ordered calibration telemetry from a drifting device.

We make three contributions. \textbf{(i)} We introduce the \emph{causal
spectral-truncation kernel}, a C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra-valued
positive-definite kernel on telemetry windows whose truncation order $n$
interpolates from a commutative instantaneous monitor ($n{=}1$) to a
noncommutative monitor that represents ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations
($n{>}1$); it is the causal, time-respecting counterpart of periodic
spectral-truncation kernels~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral} and contains the
low-rank reconstruction detector~\citep{malhotra2016lstmad} and the multivariate
ridge forecaster as commutative special cases. \textbf{(ii)} We prove and
demonstrate that noncommutativity is \emph{necessary}: on a physically motivated
correlated-decoherence drift mode whose signal lives in ordered, lagged
cross-channel structure, the commutative monitors we evaluate --- raw-level,
instantaneous-covariance and the periodic spectral-truncation kernel --- all detect
at chance, whereas the causal kernel detects at ROC-AUC~$0.90$, with a certifiable
optimal truncation order. \textbf{(iii)} We
give the supporting theory --- positive definiteness for every order, a blindness
theorem for commutative monitors, and an \emph{optimal-truncation detection
certificate} that is the unsupervised-detection analogue of the
representation--complexity bound that governs spectral-truncation kernels in
regression --- and show that on smooth marginal forecasting, where noncommutativity
is unnecessary, the kernel still attains $72\%$ horizon-eight skill over
persistence. The complete pipeline installs with \texttt{pip} and regenerates in
seconds on a single CPU.

\section{Results}

The pipeline is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}: a physically motivated
multi-qubit telemetry generator emits a multivariate calibration stream; sliding
windows are mapped, by the causal spectral-truncation kernel, into a
C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra whose truncation order $n$ sets the
noncommutativity the monitor can exploit; and two heads --- a forecaster and an
unsupervised detector --- are scored by the metric matched to their objective
(horizon-stable mean absolute error, MAE, for forecasting; the threshold-free
area under the receiver-operating-characteristic curve, ROC-AUC, for detection).
The commutative member ($n{=}1$) governs smooth marginal forecasting and
level-based drift; the noncommutative members ($n{>}1$) govern correlated,
crosstalk-like drift.

\subsection{Coherence drift is forecastable, and correlated drift hides in lagged
cross-channel structure}

We model a five-qubit processor sampled every half hour for one hundred hours.
Each \T\ trajectory follows a mean-reverting process combining a slow
qubit-dependent oscillation, a linear secular decay and Gaussian fluctuation,
with \Ttwo, gate fidelities, readout error, error-per-Clifford and
cross-resonance phase generated by coupled processes that track \T\ (Methods). The
telemetry wanders over tens of microseconds and repeatedly dips below the
\SI{50}{\micro\second} operational threshold (Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}a). The drift
is not white noise: the mean autocorrelation of detrended \T\ stays above $0.5$
for roughly seven hours before decaying (Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}b), so near-future
coherence is genuinely predictable from the recent past.

A second, qualitatively different drift mode is correlated decoherence. We model
it as a shared latent disturbance injected into several channels with
channel-specific propagation delays and \emph{variance-preserving} mixing, so that
every per-channel marginal variance and, in the stationary limit, the
instantaneous (lag-0) cross-channel covariance match nominal operation; the
disturbance lives predominantly in the \emph{ordered, lagged} cross-channel
correlation (Methods). Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}c quantifies where the resulting
signature lives: averaged over channel pairs, the nominal-versus-drift separation
is an order of magnitude smaller at lag $\tau=0$ (mean $0.008$) than at lag
$\tau=1$ (mean $0.089$), so the signal is concentrated where a commutative monitor
cannot look. A small finite-window residual remains at $\tau=0$, but the
commutative monitors we evaluate are nonetheless at chance (below), the regime in
which noncommutativity is decisive.

\subsection{Learned forecasting beats persistence; noncommutativity is
unnecessary here}

We segment telemetry into sliding windows of length $32$ that predict an
eight-step \T\ horizon, under a leak-free $80/20$ chronological split, and compare
four forecasters --- last-value persistence, climatology, an autoregressive ridge
on the \T\ history and a multivariate ridge on all seven channels (the commutative,
$n{=}1$ member of the kernel family) --- aggregated over five telemetry-generator
seeds (Methods). Forecasting is feasible and clearly structured
(Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}, Table~\ref{tab:forecast}). One step ahead the
multivariate ridge attains $1.80\pm0.09$~\si{\micro\second} MAE against
$2.44\pm0.16$~\si{\micro\second} for persistence; persistence degrades steeply with
horizon (to $6.79\pm0.18$~\si{\micro\second} at eight steps) while the learned
forecasters stay nearly flat ($1.88\pm0.06$~\si{\micro\second}), so skill over
persistence \emph{grows} from $26\%$ one step ahead to $72\%$ eight steps ahead
(Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}b, Theorem~\ref{thm:skill}).

Increasing the kernel's truncation order does not change this picture
(Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}c): the CST forecaster's eight-step skill is flat in $n$
(from $72\%$ at $n{=}1$ to $71\%$ at $n{=}4$). On smooth, mean-reverting marginal
telemetry the commutative member is already near-optimal, and the noncommutative
coordinates neither help nor hurt. This is the honest counterpart of the detection
result below: noncommutativity is decisive exactly when the drift lives in ordered
cross-channel structure, and superfluous otherwise.

\subsection{The causal spectral-truncation kernel}

A telemetry window is $X\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times C}$ ($L=32$ time steps, $C=7$
channels). Let $S$ be the causal lower shift, $(Sv)_t=v_{t-1}$. The order-$n$
causal spectral-truncation kernel represents the window inside the channel
C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra $\Mc=M_C(\mathbb{R})$ through the truncated shift
powers $\{S^\tau:0\le\tau<n\}$,
\begin{equation}
  K_n(X,Y)=\sum_{\tau=0}^{n-1}w_\tau\,X^{\top}S^{\tau}Y\in\Mc,\qquad
  K_n(X,Y)_{cc'}=\sum_{\tau=0}^{n-1}w_\tau\,\langle X_{:,c},\,S^{\tau}Y_{:,c'}\rangle,
  \label{eq:Kn}
\end{equation}
with weights $w_\tau=1/(L-\tau)$ (the telemetry is temporally centred per channel).
Each entry is a weighted sum of ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations.
Truncating the shift algebra to $n$ powers is the causal counterpart of truncating
the Fourier-multiplier algebra to $n$ modes in the spectral-truncation
construction of Hashimoto et~al.; the difference is that $S$ is nilpotent and
respects the arrow of time, so \eqref{eq:Kn} is causal and noncommutative for
$n\ge2$: the self-representation $K_n(X,X)$ is symmetric at $n{=}1$ --- the
commutative instantaneous covariance $X^{\top}X$ --- but acquires antisymmetric
lagged terms for $n\ge2$, since $S^{\top}\neq S$ does not commute with channel
selection.
The scalar realisation $k_n(X,Y)=\langle\Phi_n(X),\Phi_n(Y)\rangle$, with feature
tensor $\Phi_n(X)_{cc'\tau}=w_\tau\langle X_{:,c},S^{\tau}X_{:,c'}\rangle$, is
positive definite for every $n$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:pd}). The unsupervised detector
fits a rank-$k$ nominal subspace in this feature space and scores a window by its
residual energy off that subspace, a C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebraic
generalisation of the reconstruction detector~\citep{malhotra2016lstmad}, which it
recovers at $n{=}1$.

\subsection{Commutative monitors are blind to correlated drift; the noncommutative
kernel detects it}

We evaluate detection on the correlated-decoherence benchmark over eight randomised
$70/30$ splits (Methods). The result is a clean separation
(Fig.~\ref{fig:detection}, Table~\ref{tab:detection}). At truncation order $n{=}1$
the kernel is commutative and detects at ROC-AUC $0.51\pm0.04$ --- chance level,
the empirical content of Theorem~\ref{thm:blind}, because the benchmark matches the
marginals and the (population) instantaneous covariance the commutative member
reads. At $n{=}2$ the kernel becomes noncommutative and
ROC-AUC jumps to $0.90\pm0.02$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:detection}a), the single largest
effect in the study. Increasing $n$ further degrades detection monotonically --- to
$0.83$ at $n{=}3$, $0.65$ at $n{=}8$ and toward chance as $n\to L$ --- because each
added lag contributes only noise coordinates beyond the informative lag, diluting
the fixed-rank nominal subspace. Both endpoints (commutative $n{=}1$ and
over-complete $n\to L$) sit at chance, with a sharp interior optimum at $n{=}2$:
this is the optimal-truncation certificate of Theorem~\ref{thm:optn} made visible,
the detection analogue of the representation--complexity trade-off that governs
spectral-truncation kernels in regression~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral}. The
$(n,k)$ map (Fig.~\ref{fig:detection}b) shows the effect is robust across
bottleneck rank: the entire $n{=}1$ row is at chance, the $n{=}2$ row is bright
across all ranks.

The comparison that matters for novelty is against other kernels
(Fig.~\ref{fig:detection}c, Table~\ref{tab:detection}). On this benchmark the
raw-level reconstruction detector scores $0.49\pm0.03$, the commutative kernel
$0.46\pm0.02$, and --- tellingly --- the \emph{periodic} spectral-truncation kernel
of Hashimoto et~al., applied unchanged to these windows, scores $0.47\pm0.03$: all
three are at chance. Only the causal spectral-truncation kernel separates the
classes, at $0.84\pm0.02$ at matched bottleneck rank and $0.90$ at its optimum. The
periodic kernel fails not because spectral truncation is wrong but because its
\emph{circular} truncation smears the causal delay signature across the window
boundary; the causal lower-shift truncation aligns exactly with the propagation
delays that define the drift. Causality, not truncation alone, is what makes the
kernel see the event.

\subsection{Operating characteristic and over-complete collapse}

Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}a contrasts the receiver-operating characteristics directly: on
a held-out split the causal kernel at $n{=}2$ achieves AUC $0.86$ while the
commutative kernel traces the chance diagonal (AUC $0.46$). Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}b

the drift coordinates as faithfully as the nominal ones cannot separate them.

% ── Theory ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Theory: a C\texorpdfstring{$^\ast$}{*}-algebraic kernel whose
truncation order certifies detectability}

We make the kernel precise and prove the facts that organise the empirics: it is a
positive-definite, C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra-valued kernel for every order
$n$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:pd}); the commutative $n{=}1$ member is provably blind to
drift living in ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations, which the
noncommutative $n{>}1$ member resolves (Theorem~\ref{thm:blind}); the
reconstruction detector built on the kernel has a non-monotone, certifiable optimal
truncation order (Theorem~\ref{thm:optn}); ROC-AUC is the scale-free yardstick
throughout (Proposition~\ref{prop:auc}); and on the forecasting side, mean
reversion converts decaying autocorrelation into horizon-growing skill
(Theorem~\ref{thm:skill}).

\begin{theorem}[Positive definiteness of the causal C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-kernel]
\label{thm:pd}
For every order $n\ge1$ and weights $w_\tau\ge0$, the map $K_n$ in
\eqref{eq:Kn} is a C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra-valued positive-definite
kernel: for any windows $X_1,\dots,X_m$ the block matrix
$[K_n(X_i,X_j)]_{ij}$ is positive semidefinite over $\Mc$, and the scalar kernel
$k_n$ is positive definite in the ordinary sense. The self-representation
$\Phi_n(X)$ is symmetric under $c\leftrightarrow c'$ at $n{=}1$ (the commutative
instantaneous covariance) and, for $n\ge2$, acquires antisymmetric lagged
coordinates that the commutative member cannot represent.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define the lifted causal feature operator
$\Psi_n(X)=\big(\sqrt{w_0}\,X,\ \sqrt{w_1}\,S^{\top}X,\ \dots,\
\sqrt{w_{n-1}}\,(S^{\top})^{\,n-1}X\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{nL\times C}$, stacking the
back-shifted windows. Since $\langle X_{:,c},S^{\tau}Y_{:,c'}\rangle
=\big((S^{\top})^{\tau}X\big)_{:,c}^{\top}\big((S^{\top})^{\tau}Y\big)_{:,c'}$, the
kernel factorises as
$K_n(X,Y)=\sum_{\tau}w_\tau\,(S^{\top\tau}X)^{\top}(S^{\top\tau}Y)
=\Psi_n(X)^{\top}\Psi_n(Y)$. Hence for any $c_1,\dots,c_m\in\mathbb{R}^C$,
\[
  \sum_{i,j}c_i^{\top}K_n(X_i,X_j)c_j
  =\Big\|\sum_i \Psi_n(X_i)c_i\Big\|_F^2\ \ge 0,
\]
the defining inequality for an $\Mc$-valued positive-definite kernel; the scalar
statement is the case $C{=}1$ applied to the vectorised feature
$\operatorname{vec}\Phi_n$, whose Gram is $k_n$. At $n{=}1$,
$\Phi_1(X)_{cc'}=\langle X_{:,c},X_{:,c'}\rangle$ is symmetric under
$c\leftrightarrow c'$; for $\tau\ge1$, $S^{\tau}\neq S^{\tau\top}$, so
$\Phi_n(X)_{cc'\tau}=\langle X_{:,c},S^{\tau}X_{:,c'}\rangle\neq
\langle X_{:,c'},S^{\tau}X_{:,c}\rangle=\Phi_n(X)_{c'c\tau}$ whenever the lagged
cross-correlation is direction-dependent --- the noncommutativity.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Commutative monitors are blind to noncommutative drift]
\label{thm:blind}
Let nominal and drift windows be drawn from distributions $P_0,P_1$ with matched
per-channel marginals and matched instantaneous cross-covariance,
$\mathbb{E}_{P_0}[\Phi_1]=\mathbb{E}_{P_1}[\Phi_1]$, but a separated lagged
coordinate $\Delta_{\tau^\star}:=\mathbb{E}_{P_1}[\Phi_{\cdot\,\cdot\,\tau^\star}]
-\mathbb{E}_{P_0}[\Phi_{\cdot\,\cdot\,\tau^\star}]\neq0$ for some $\tau^\star\ge1$.
Then every detector that is a function of the order-$1$ (commutative) features
$\Phi_1$ has zero first-moment separation and hence chance-level mean
discriminability, whereas the order-$n$ causal kernel with $n>\tau^\star$ has
nonzero separation in a coordinate it represents; its mean reconstruction-score
gap is bounded below by the energy of $\Delta_{\tau^\star}$ outside the rank-$k$
nominal subspace.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
A monitor measurable with respect to $\Phi_1$ can separate the classes in mean
only through $\mathbb{E}_{P_1}[\Phi_1]-\mathbb{E}_{P_0}[\Phi_1]$, which is $0$ by
hypothesis; thus its expected score is identical under $P_0$ and $P_1$, giving a
mean separation of $0$ and, for the centred linear detector, ROC-AUC $=\tfrac12$ in
the population by the Mann--Whitney identity (Proposition~\ref{prop:auc}) applied
to a score with equal class means and, to first order, equal dispersion. The
order-$n$ feature map with $n>\tau^\star$ contains the coordinate
$\Phi_{\cdot\,\cdot\,\tau^\star}$, on which the class means differ by
$\Delta_{\tau^\star}\neq0$. Writing $P_k$ for the rank-$k$ nominal-subspace
projector in feature space, the reconstruction score is
$s(X)=\norm{(I-P_k)(\Phi_n(X)-\bar\Phi)}^2$ and
$\mathbb{E}_{P_1}[s]-\mathbb{E}_{P_0}[s]\ge\norm{(I-P_k)\Delta_{\tau^\star}}^2$ up to
cross terms that vanish when $\Delta_{\tau^\star}$ is centred against the nominal
mean, so any nonzero component of $\Delta_{\tau^\star}$ outside the nominal subspace
yields strictly positive separation, hence ROC-AUC $>\tfrac12$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal truncation: a detection certificate]
\label{thm:optn}
Let $e_\tau=\norm{\Delta_\tau}^2$ be the drift energy at lag $\tau$, with
$e_\tau=0$ beyond a maximal informative lag $\tau_{\max}$, and suppose each added
lag contributes $C^2$ noise coordinates of bounded variance to the fixed rank-$k$
nominal subspace. The order-$n$ detector's mean separation-to-noise ratio is
$\rho(n)=\big(\sum_{\tau<n}e_\tau\big)^{1/2}\big/\big(\sigma^2(C^2 n-k)_+\big)^{1/2}$.
Then $\rho$ is unimodal in $n$ with an interior optimum $n^\star$, and its detection
ROC-AUC, a strictly increasing function of $\rho$, is unimodal with the same
optimum; both the commutative endpoint ($n{=}1$, $\rho=0$) and the over-complete
limit ($n\to L$, $\rho\to0$) are at chance. The optimum $n^\star$ is the smallest
$n$ at which the marginal relative signal gain falls below the marginal relative
noise cost, $e_{n}/\!\sum_{\tau<n}e_\tau<\big(N(n{+}1)-N(n)\big)/N(n)$; in
particular $n^\star=2$ whenever the drift energy is concentrated at the first
informative lag ($e_1$ dominant), and $n^\star$ grows as the energy profile
spreads to higher lags, never exceeding $\tau_{\max}+1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $E(n)=\sum_{\tau<n}e_\tau$ (non-decreasing, constant for $n>\tau_{\max}+1$)
and $N(n)=\sigma^2(C^2 n-k)_+$ (strictly increasing once $C^2 n>k$). Then
$\rho(n)^2=E(n)/N(n)$, and $\rho(n{+}1)^2>\rho(n)^2$ iff
$E(n{+}1)/E(n)>N(n{+}1)/N(n)$, i.e.\ iff the relative signal increment
$e_n/E(n)$ exceeds the relative noise increment $(N(n{+}1)-N(n))/N(n)$. The
right-hand side is positive for all $n$ (the noise floor always grows), while the
left-hand side is $0$ once $n>\tau_{\max}+1$ ($e_n=0$) and is non-increasing under a
concentrated energy profile; hence the inequality holds on an initial range and
fails thereafter, so $\rho$ rises then falls --- unimodal with a single interior
optimum $n^\star$, the first $n$ at which it fails. If $e_1$ dominates, the test
already fails at $n{=}2$ (the small later increments $e_2,\dots$ cannot offset the
$\sqrt{n}$ noise growth), giving $n^\star=2$; broader energy profiles push $n^\star$
up to at most $\tau_{\max}+1$. At $n{=}1$, $\rho=0$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:blind}; as
$n\to L$, $\rho\to0$ by dilution --- the over-complete chance-level collapse.
\end{proof}

\noindent Theorem~\ref{thm:optn} is the unsupervised-detection counterpart of the
generalisation bound that governs spectral-truncation kernels in
regression~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral}: there the truncation order trades
representation power against Rademacher complexity; here it trades captured drift
energy against the dimension of the nominal subspace. The correlated-decoherence
mode concentrates its energy at the first informative lag
(Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}c), so the certificate predicts the empirical optimum at
$n^\star=2$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:detection}a). The classical low-rank over-completeness
collapse~\citep{malhotra2016lstmad} is recovered as the rank axis of the same
certificate.

\begin{proposition}[Threshold-free ranking and scale invariance]
\label{prop:auc}
For any score $s$ and independent draws of a drift window $X^{+}$ and a nominal
window $X^{-}$, the ROC area obeys the Mann--Whitney identity
$\mathrm{AUC}=\Pr[s(X^{+})>s(X^{-})]+\tfrac12\Pr[s(X^{+})=s(X^{-})]$.
Consequently $\mathrm{AUC}$ is invariant under every strictly increasing
reparametrisation of $s$, and in particular under per-feature affine rescaling of
the telemetry; a constant score gives $\mathrm{AUC}=\tfrac12$ exactly.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $F_{+},F_{-}$ be the score CDFs under the drift and nominal classes. For a
threshold $\tau$ flagging $s>\tau$, $\mathrm{TPR}(\tau)=1-F_{+}(\tau)$ and
$\mathrm{FPR}(\tau)=1-F_{-}(\tau)$, and
$\mathrm{AUC}=\int_0^1\mathrm{TPR}\,\mathrm{d}(\mathrm{FPR})$. Substituting
$u=\mathrm{FPR}(\tau)$,
$\mathrm{AUC}=\int\Pr[s(X^{+})>\tau]\,\mathrm{d}F_{-}(\tau)=\Pr[s(X^{+})>s(X^{-})]$
for a continuous score; atoms contribute $\tfrac12\Pr[s(X^{+})=s(X^{-})]$ via the
trapezoidal interpolation of each jump, giving the identity (the normalised
Mann--Whitney $U$). Strict monotone maps preserve all order and tie events, hence
$\mathrm{AUC}$; a constant score makes every comparison a tie, so
$\mathrm{AUC}=0+\tfrac12\cdot1=\tfrac12$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Horizon-growing forecast skill]
\label{thm:skill}
Let $\{x_t\}$ be weakly stationary, zero-mean, with variance $\gamma_0>0$ and
autocorrelation $\rho(h)=\operatorname{Corr}(x_{t+h},x_t)$. Persistence
$\hat{x}_{t+h}=x_t$ has mean-squared $h$-step risk
$R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)=2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$, while the optimal single-lag linear
forecast $\hat{x}_{t+h}=\rho(h)x_t$ has risk
$R_\star(h)=\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)\le R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)$, so the relative skill is
$S(h)=1-R_\star/R_{\mathrm{pers}}=(1-\rho(h))/2$. Where $\rho$ is non-increasing,
$S(h)$ is non-decreasing, bounded above by $\tfrac12$. For a first-order
autoregressive process $\rho(h)=\phi^{\,h}$, the single measured number
$\phi=\rho(1)$ fixes the whole skill curve.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With $\hat{x}_{t+h}=ax_t$, $\mathbb{E}[(x_{t+h}-ax_t)^2]
=\gamma_0(1-2a\rho(h)+a^2)$, a strictly convex parabola minimised at
$a^\star=\rho(h)$, giving $R_\star=\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$; $a{=}1$ gives
$R_{\mathrm{pers}}=2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$. Dividing,
$R_\star/R_{\mathrm{pers}}=(1+\rho(h))/2$, so $S(h)=(1-\rho(h))/2$, which is
strictly decreasing in $\rho$; wherever $\rho(h)$ decreases in $h$, $S(h)$
increases, and $S\le\tfrac12$ on $\rho\in[0,1]$. For an AR(1) process iterating
the recursion gives $\operatorname{Cov}(x_{t+h},x_t)=\phi^{\,h}\gamma_0$, hence
$\rho(h)=\phi^{\,h}$ and $S(h)=(1-\phi^{\,h})/2$, increasing from $0$ toward
$\tfrac12$.
\end{proof}

\noindent Theorem~\ref{thm:skill} is a lower bound using only the most recent
sample; the multivariate ridge (the commutative $n{=}1$ member) exceeds it by
exploiting the full $32$-step, seven-channel history, which is why its measured
eight-step skill reaches $72\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}). The noncommutative
coordinates add nothing here because the marginal forecasting structure is already
linear and instantaneous --- the precise complement of the detection regime, where
they are everything.

% ── Discussion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

Our results recast quantum-hardware reliability monitoring as operator-algebraic
learning and deliver a single operational rule: \emph{match the kernel's
noncommutativity to the geometry of the drift}. Level-based and instantaneous
monitors --- including the periodic spectral-truncation kernel from which our
construction descends --- are blind to correlated decoherence that lives in
ordered, lagged cross-channel structure, the very failure mode quantum error
correction is least able to absorb. The causal spectral-truncation kernel sees it,
and an elementary certificate (Theorem~\ref{thm:optn}) both explains why a small
truncation order is optimal and predicts where the optimum sits. The same kernel,
at its commutative order, recovers established forecasting and low-rank detection
as special cases, so the family is a strict generalisation rather than a competitor
to them. Deep sequence-model monitors --- recurrent~\citep{hochreiter1997lstm,
cho2014gru} and attention-based~\citep{vaswani2017attention} architectures studied
elsewhere for telemetry~\citep{lim2021forecasting,wen2023transformers} --- are
complementary, but require training and many parameters; the kernel is training-free
at inference, deterministic, and CPU-reproducible in seconds.

Several limitations bound these claims. The telemetry generator, though physically
motivated, is a model rather than measured device data, and the
correlated-decoherence mode is a controlled construction chosen to isolate the
noncommutative effect; archival calibration logs from operating processors and a
standard anomaly-detection benchmark~\citep{lavin2015nab} are the natural next
validations. The kernel as presented is linear in its feature map; composing it
with an outer nonlinearity, and pairing the forecaster with a recalibration policy
under an explicit cost model and uncertainty-aware
predictions~\citep{gal2016dropout}, are clear extensions. Because the entire
pipeline is open, hardware-agnostic and fast to reproduce, it is positioned to
serve as a common substrate for that work.

% ── Methods ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}

\paragraph{Telemetry generation.}
A synthetic multi-qubit dataset is produced for $Q=5$ qubits over $N=200$
half-hour steps. For qubit $q$ at time $t$ (hours), the relaxation time is
$T_1(q,t)=\max(10,\,80+20\sin(2\pi t/48+0.8q)-0.05\,t+\varepsilon)$ with
$\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,2^2)$, combining a slow qubit-dependent oscillation,
secular decay and Gaussian noise. The dephasing time is $T_2=0.9\,T_1\,\eta$,
$\eta\sim\mathcal{N}(1,0.04^2)$; single- and two-qubit gate fidelities,
readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and cross-resonance phase are
generated by analogous bounded processes with small secular trends. A binary drift
label marks any step with $T_1<\SI{50}{\micro\second}$ or single-qubit fidelity
below $0.998$. Generation is fully seeded.

\paragraph{Correlated-decoherence benchmark.}
The detection benchmark contrasts nominal windows against
\emph{correlated-decoherence} windows that share the same physical base trajectory
and the same per-channel noise scales. The drift class injects a single shared
\emph{white} latent into four channels (\T, \Ttwo, readout error, cross-resonance
phase) at channel-specific integer delays $(0,1,2,3)$ with variance-preserving
mixing
$x_c\leftarrow B_c+\sigma_c(\sqrt{1-a^2}\,\varepsilon_c+a\,u_{t-\delta_c})$,
$a=1$. Because the latent is white, every per-channel marginal variance and every
temporal autocorrelation are left unchanged, and the population lag-0
cross-covariance ($\tau=0$, $\delta_c\neq\delta_{c'}$) is zero in both classes;
the perturbation is the ordered lag-$\tau$ cross-channel covariance
($\tau=\delta_{c'}-\delta_c\ge1$). Per-window temporal centering over $L=32$ leaves
a small finite-window residual at $\tau=0$, but the drift energy is concentrated at
$\tau\ge1$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}c), and the commutative monitors we evaluate ---
raw-level, instantaneous-covariance and the periodic spectral-truncation kernel ---
are all empirically at chance, exposed only by the causal noncommutative ($n\ge2$)
kernel --- the empirical content of Theorem~\ref{thm:blind}.

\paragraph{Causal spectral-truncation kernel.}
A window $X\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times C}$ ($L=32$, $C=7$) is temporally centred per
channel and mapped to the feature tensor
$\Phi_n(X)_{cc'\tau}=\langle X_{:,c},S^{\tau}X_{:,c'}\rangle/(L-\tau)$ for
$\tau=0,\dots,n-1$, with $S$ the causal lower shift; the operator-valued kernel is
\eqref{eq:Kn} and the scalar kernel is the Hilbert--Schmidt inner product of these
features. The commutative member ($n{=}1$) keeps only the instantaneous covariance;
$n{>}1$ adds ordered lagged cross-channel coordinates. The \emph{periodic}
spectral-truncation baseline maps each channel to the $n\times n$ Toeplitz matrix
of its truncated Fourier coefficients and uses the ordered cross-channel products
of those matrices, the construction of Hashimoto
et~al.~\citep{hashimoto2024spectral} applied unchanged.

\paragraph{Forecasting.}
We compare persistence (carry the last \T\ forward), climatology (the
training-mean horizon trajectory), an autoregressive ridge on the $32$-step \T\
history, and a multivariate ridge on the flattened $32\times7$ window (ridge
penalty $\alpha=5$; the commutative $n{=}1$ kernel member), under a leak-free
per-qubit $80/20$ chronological split, scored by MAE per horizon step over five
generator seeds; skill is $1-\mathrm{MAE}/\mathrm{MAE}_{\text{persistence}}$. The
CST forecaster augments the window with the order-$n$ causal features before the
ridge solve; its eight-step skill versus $n$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}c) is
averaged over the same five seeds.

\paragraph{Detection.}
The unsupervised detector standardises the kernel feature map on nominal training
windows, fits a rank-$k$ principal subspace, and scores each test window by its
mean squared reconstruction residual outside that subspace. Detection quality is
ROC-AUC against the drift labels, over eight randomised $70/30$ splits; the
detector is fit on nominal windows only, so randomisation cannot leak a label it
never uses. The truncation sweep reports, per order $n$, the best ROC-AUC over
bottleneck ranks $k\in\{1,2,3,5,8,12,16\}$; the kernel comparison
(Table~\ref{tab:detection}) fixes $k=8$.

\paragraph{Reproducibility and compute.}
The analyses run entirely on a single CPU and regenerate in seconds. Every figure
and table is computed live from the installed \texttt{qdriftforecast} package by
\texttt{make\_paper\_figures.py} (the installed \texttt{qdrift-reproduce} entry
point); reported uncertainties are mean~$\pm$~standard deviation across the $n=5$
generator seeds (forecasting) or $n=8$ randomised splits (detection). Determinism
is byte-level: seeds and the build epoch are pinned, and the vector PDF output is
fixed across runs.

\section*{Supplementary Information}

\noindent This appendix is a self-contained derivation of every concept the
released code implements, written for a reader with linear algebra and basic
probability but no prior exposure to quantum-hardware telemetry, operator-algebraic
kernels or the estimators used here. Every symbol that appears in the code is
defined, and each subsection points to the source file (under
\texttt{submission/code/}) that realises it. The whole artefact is CPU-only and
deterministic: every number in the paper is a fixed function of integer seeds.

\paragraph{Source-file map.} All paths are relative to \texttt{submission/code/}:
\texttt{qdriftforecast/data.py} (telemetry generator, correlated-drift benchmark,
windowing, splitting); \texttt{qdriftforecast/kernels.py} (the causal
spectral-truncation kernel, its operator- and scalar-valued forms, and the
periodic baseline); \texttt{qdriftforecast/detection.py} (the reconstruction
detector, truncation sweep, $(n,k)$ grid and kernel comparison);
\texttt{qdriftforecast/forecasting.py} (forecasters); \texttt{make\_\allowbreak
paper\_\allowbreak figures.py} (all figures and table bodies); and
\texttt{generated\_\allowbreak data/*.json} (the numbers behind each panel).

\subsection*{S1. The problem in one paragraph}
A quantum processor is not static: its coherence times, gate fidelities, readout
error and coupling parameters drift over hours, eroding the error budget that
quantum error correction depends on, so an operator must decide \emph{when} a
device has drifted and \emph{which} intervals warrant recalibration. We pose two
tasks on the multivariate telemetry: \emph{forecast} near-future coherence, and
\emph{detect} drifted windows without labels. The novel finding is that one
geometry of drift --- correlated decoherence in ordered, lagged cross-channel
structure --- leaves the commutative monitors we evaluate at chance and is
detectable only by a noncommutative one.

\subsection*{S2. Computation model: telemetry as arrays}
\emph{(\texttt{qdriftforecast/data.py}.)} A telemetry record is a finite table;
each row is a \texttt{(timestamp, qubit\_id)} pair holding measured or simulated
features. The seven channels (\texttt{FEATURE\_COLS}) are \texttt{T1\_us},
\texttt{T2\_us}, \texttt{gate\_fidelity\_1q}, \texttt{gate\_fidelity\_2q},
\texttt{readout\_error}, \texttt{gate\_error\_per\_clifford} and
\texttt{cross\_resonance\_phase\_rad}. All results are deterministic functions of
these arrays and fixed integer seeds.

\subsection*{S3. The physics of the generator}
\emph{(\texttt{generate\_synthetic\_dataset}.)} For qubit $q$ at time $t$ the
relaxation time is
$T_1(q,t)=\max(10,\,80+20\sin(2\pi t/48+0.8q)-0.05\,t+\varepsilon)$,
$\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,2^2)$, superposing a $48$-hour oscillation
(recalibration-like cycling, phase $0.8q$ decorrelating qubits), a secular decay
(ageing) and Gaussian fluctuation. The other channels track $T_1$ by construction.
A binary drift label marks any step with $T_1<\SI{50}{\micro\second}$ or
single-qubit fidelity below $0.998$. Because the deterministic part is smooth and
the noise small, the detrended series is weakly stationary with a slowly decaying
autocorrelation, the structure S8 turns into forecast skill.

\subsection*{S4. The correlated-decoherence benchmark, and why commutative
monitors are at chance}
\emph{(\texttt{generate\_correlated\_drift\_windows}.)} Both classes share the same
base trajectory $B$ and noise scales $\sigma$. The drift class adds a shared white
latent $u$ to four channels at delays $\delta_c\in\{0,1,2,3\}$ with
variance-preserving mixing
$x_c=B_c+\sigma_c(\sqrt{1-a^2}\,\varepsilon_c+a\,u_{t-\delta_c})$. Because $u$ is
white: (i) each channel's marginal variance is $\sigma_c^2$ in both classes; (ii)
each channel's temporal autocorrelation is unchanged (a sum of independent whites
is white); (iii) the \emph{population} lag-0 cross-covariance between two injected
channels is
$a^2\sigma_c\sigma_{c'}\mathbb{E}[u_{t-\delta_c}u_{t-\delta_{c'}}]=0$ for
$\delta_c\neq\delta_{c'}$, matching nominal. The signal energy lies in the
lag-$\tau$ cross-covariance with $\tau=\delta_{c'}-\delta_c\ge1$. Two caveats keep
the account honest: per-window temporal centering over the finite $L=32$ window
leaves a small $O(1/L)$ residual in the centred lag-0 cross-covariance of the
drift class (so the matching is exact only in the stationary limit), and
Theorem~\ref{thm:blind} is a statement about \emph{first-moment} discriminability,
not about every conceivable nonlinear statistic. What we verify empirically is the
operative claim: the commutative monitors we evaluate --- the raw-level detector,
the commutative ($n{=}1$) kernel and the periodic spectral-truncation kernel ---
are all at chance ($n{=}1$ ROC-AUC $0.51$), because the drift energy is
concentrated in the ordered, lagged cross-channel correlations they do not
represent (Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}c).

\subsection*{S5. The causal spectral-truncation kernel, from scratch}
\emph{(\texttt{qdriftforecast/kernels.py}.)} Centre each channel of a window in
time. For lags $\tau=0,\dots,n-1$ form the ordered cross-channel correlation
$\Phi_n(X)_{cc'\tau}=\frac{1}{L-\tau}\sum_{t\ge\tau}X_{t,c}X_{t-\tau,c'}$, the
matrix elements of $X^{\top}S^{\tau}X$ with $S$ the causal lower shift. The
operator-valued kernel $K_n(X,Y)=\sum_{\tau<n}w_\tau X^{\top}S^{\tau}Y\in M_C$ is an
element of the channel C\textsuperscript{$\ast$}-algebra; the scalar kernel is
$\langle\Phi_n(X),\Phi_n(Y)\rangle$. At $n{=}1$ this is the symmetric instantaneous
covariance (commutative); for $n\ge2$ the lagged terms are asymmetric in $(c,c')$
because $S^{\top}\neq S$, which is the noncommutativity. Truncating the shift
algebra to $n$ powers is the causal analogue of truncating the Fourier-multiplier
algebra to $n$ modes in the spectral-truncation construction of Hashimoto
et~al.; the periodic baseline (\texttt{periodic\_truncation\_feature\_map})
implements that original torus construction for comparison.

\subsection*{S6. The detector and the optimal-truncation certificate}
\emph{(\texttt{qdriftforecast/detection.py}.)} Standardise the kernel features on
nominal windows, fit a rank-$k$ principal subspace, and score a test window by its
residual energy outside it. Increasing the truncation order $n$ both captures more
drift signal (good) and adds $C^2$ noise coordinates per lag (bad); the
separation-to-noise ratio is therefore unimodal in $n$, peaking just past the
informative lag (Theorem~\ref{thm:optn}). Empirically the peak is at $n{=}2$,
ROC-AUC $0.90$; the commutative endpoint and the over-complete limit are both at
chance, and the rank axis recovers the classical low-rank over-completeness
collapse.

\subsection*{S7. Metrics: MAE for forecasting, ROC-AUC for detection}
Forecasting uses mean absolute error per horizon step, reported per step so the
widening gap over persistence is visible, with skill
$1-\mathrm{MAE}/\mathrm{MAE}_{\text{pers}}$. Detection uses ROC-AUC, which by the
Mann--Whitney identity (Proposition~\ref{prop:auc}) equals the probability that a
random drift window outranks a random nominal one, is invariant under per-feature
rescaling, and equals $\tfrac12$ for a constant score --- the chance level that the
commutative and over-complete monitors attain.

\subsection*{S8. Why forecast skill grows with the horizon}
This is Theorem~\ref{thm:skill}. For a weakly stationary process with
autocorrelation $\rho(h)$, persistence risk is $2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$ and the best
single-lag predictor's risk is $\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$, so skill is
$(1-\rho(h))/2$, increasing as $\rho$ decays. The multivariate ridge uses the full
history and exceeds this single-lag bound, reaching $72\%$ at horizon eight; the
noncommutative coordinates add nothing because the marginal structure is already
linear and instantaneous (S5).

\subsection*{S10. What the result means, and what it does not}
A small, training-free, noncommutative kernel detects a correlated-decoherence
drift that every commutative monitor --- including the periodic spectral-truncation
kernel --- misses entirely, and a single truncation order certifies how much
noncommutativity to use. Coherence forecasting succeeds where autocorrelation
persists, and there noncommutativity is unnecessary. No quantum-hardware
superiority claim is made: the telemetry is a physically motivated model and the
correlated-decoherence mode is a controlled construction chosen to isolate the
noncommutative effect. The value delivered is a reusable, leakage-controlled,
CPU-reproducible, operator-algebraic foundation for quantum-processor reliability
monitoring, and a demonstration that the geometry of drift dictates the
noncommutativity a monitor must have.
