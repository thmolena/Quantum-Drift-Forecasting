Objective-Aware Sequence Modelling for Drift Forecasting and Anomaly Detection in Quantum Hardware

Molena Huynh  ·  North Carolina State University  ·  molena.huynh@jmp.com

Qubit coherence is forecastable, and the strongest sequence model varies with the monitoring task. We cast superconducting-qubit reliability as a multivariate, mean-reverting forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem and, under one leakage-free protocol, establish that the leading detector is governed by the objective rather than by a universally dominant architecture. Background & Motivation

Qubit-parameter drift as a sequence-modelling problem

A superconducting processor drifts continuously. The relaxation time $T_1$, the dephasing time $T_2$, gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and tunable-coupling parameters all vary with temperature, flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history. This drift is consequential at both ends of the technology roadmap: in the NISQ regime it caps usable circuit depth, and in the fault-tolerant regime it erodes the premise that physical error rates stay below threshold long enough for error correction to suppress logical errors.

At hundreds to thousands of qubits, two operational decisions exceed the reach of manual inspection: identifying the instant a device leaves specification, and isolating the intervals that warrant recalibration. The calibration stream is a multivariate stochastic process with slow periodic structure, secular degradation and stochastic fluctuation, so forecasting it and flagging off-manifold windows is squarely a time-series learning task. The open gap is decision-oriented guidance: which model family to deploy, at what parameter cost, and for which objective.

Contributions & Novelty

Principal contributions

Three contributions follow, each reproducible end-to-end on commodity CPU hardware.

  1. A leakage-free, objective-aware benchmark. We cast quantum-hardware reliability as a joint forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem over a physically motivated, reproducible telemetry generator, and release one leakage-free benchmark that scores four sequence-model families on this task and on three heterogeneous real-world regimes.
  2. Model selection is objective-dependent. Architecture rankings reverse across objectives: the GRU is a parameter-efficient generalist — the sole model to reach non-trivial incident detection — while the Transformer is a specialist that leads on periodic, calibration-like signals and generalises weakly elsewhere.
  3. A reconstruction detector with a provable control parameter. We introduce a low-rank reconstruction drift detector whose bottleneck rank is the single knob separating drift from nominal behaviour — an interpretable monitor that, at inference, evaluates a fixed linear projection and is backed by an empirical-optimality theorem.
Mathematics & Method

The model, the estimators, and their guarantees

Two theorems account for the two headline findings: the growth of forecast skill with horizon, and the peak of detection at low reconstruction rank.

Drift generator (physics)

A mean-reverting multivariate telemetry process

For qubit $q$ at time $t$ (hours), the relaxation time is a sum of three terms — a 48-hour recalibration-like oscillation, secular ageing, and Gaussian fluctuation:

$$ T_1(q,t) = \max\!\Big(10,\; 80 + 20\sin\!\big(\tfrac{2\pi t}{48} + 0.8q\big) - 0.05\,t + \varepsilon\Big), \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2^2). $$

The dephasing time $T_2 = 0.9\,T_1\,\eta$ tracks $T_1$, and the remaining channels are analogous bounded processes. A window is labelled out-of-specification when $T_1 < 50~\mu\text{s}$ or single-qubit fidelity $< 0.998$. The configuration is $Q=5$ qubits, $N=200$ half-hour steps (100 h), and seven channels.

Leak-free windowing

Sliding windows, chronological splits

Each series is cut into windows of length $L=32$ that predict an $H=8$-step $T_1$ horizon, pooled across qubits. Chronological splitting rules out leakage: $70/15/15$ for the sequence-model benchmark, per-qubit $80/20$ for the forecasting baselines, and randomised $70/30$ for the detector — the last admissible only because the detector is fit on nominal windows alone. Scalers are estimated on training data and applied forward.

Ridge closed form

Multivariate ridge forecaster

Take flattened windows as the rows of $X$ and 8-step horizons as the rows of $Y$. Ridge regression solves $\min_W \lVert XW - Y\rVert_2^2 + \alpha\lVert W\rVert_2^2$ in closed form ($\alpha=5$):

$$ W^\star = (X^\top X + \alpha I)^{-1} X^\top Y. $$

The $\alpha I$ term keeps the normal equations invertible even when the channels are correlated. The baselines are persistence, climatology, and AR-ridge (the 32-step $T_1$ history alone).

Horizon-skill theorem

Skill widens with horizon

For a weakly stationary, zero-mean process with variance $\gamma_0$ and autocorrelation $\rho(h)$, persistence incurs risk $2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$ and the optimal single-lag predictor risk $\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$, so the relative skill is

$$ S(h) = 1 - \frac{R_\star(h)}{R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)} = \frac{1-\rho(h)}{2}. $$

As $\rho(h)$ decays, $S(h)$ rises monotonically toward $\tfrac12$. This bound uses only the most recent sample; the multivariate ridge beats it by reading the full 32-step, 7-channel history, and measured skill reaches 72% at horizon 8.

PCA reconstruction detector

Drift as off-manifold residual energy

We take the top-$k$ right singular vectors $V_k$ of the centred nominal matrix and project with $P_k = V_kV_k^\top$. By an empirical-optimality theorem (Rayleigh–Ritz), the drift score $s_k(x) = \lVert x - xP_k\rVert_2^2$ is the smallest residual any rank-$k$ linear monitor fitted on nominal data can attain. At $k = d$ the projection becomes $P_k = I$, the score is constant, and detection falls to chance — a theorem, not a tuning artefact.

ROC-AUC (Mann–Whitney)

The scale-free yardstick for detection

For independent drift $X^{+}$ and nominal $X^{-}$ windows, $\mathrm{AUC} = \Pr[s(X^{+}) > s(X^{-})] + \tfrac12\Pr[s(X^{+}) = s(X^{-})]$. Because this quantity is unchanged by any strictly increasing reparametrisation of $s$ — including per-feature affine rescaling — AUC, not MAE or RMSE, anchors the cross-domain comparison.

Experimental Design & Statistics

Provenance of the reported numbers

The benchmark keeps replicated quantities distinct from single-run point estimates and reports, for each monitoring objective, the metric matched to it.

Replication & uncertainty

The meaning of ± here

Forecasting metrics are averaged over n = 5 generator seeds and the reconstruction detector over n = 8 randomised splits, with every uncertainty reported as mean ± standard deviation. The deep sequence-model metrics (the thermal and cross-domain tables) are single deterministic, seeded runs given as point estimates, labelled as such and reported as point values rather than confidence intervals.

Leakage control

Train/test windowing

Chronological splits keep future intervals out of training: $70/15/15$ for the sequence-model benchmark, per-qubit $80/20$ for the baselines, and randomised $70/30$ for the detector (fit on nominal windows alone). Windows are length 32, the horizon is 8, and all are pooled across five qubits.

Model families compared

Four sequence models + baselines

Four architectures are compared: a single-layer Elman RNN, a two-layer LSTM, a two-layer GRU, and an encoder-only Transformer (pre-LN, $d_\text{model}=128$, 4 heads, 3 layers). Each emits an 8-step forecast and a drift logit, trained with AdamW under a cosine schedule on $\mathrm{MSE}+\mathrm{BCE}$. The forecasting baselines are persistence, climatology, AR-ridge, and multivariate ridge.

ROC-AUC methodology

Threshold-free ranking

Detection is scored two ways — F1 at the default threshold and threshold-free ROC-AUC (Mann–Whitney $U$) — because the two can disagree: a model may rank windows well (high AUC) yet flag nothing at a fixed operating point (zero F1). The cross-domain evaluation uses three NAB datasets as stand-ins for periodic, failure-bearing and irregular regimes.

Unsupervised drift detector

Low rank separates drift

Rank-3 reconstruction separates the regimes at ROC-AUC 0.72 on a held-out split (0.70 ± 0.05 over 8 randomised splits). Sweeping the bottleneck rank, detection is strongest at low rank (0.87 ± 0.02 at rank 1) and decays toward chance as the code grows over-complete and begins memorising drifted windows.

Drift lives off a low-rank manifold; the rank is the false-alarm control parameter.
Key statistical comparison

F1 and ROC-AUC disagree by design

A model can rank intervals well (high ROC-AUC) while flagging nothing at a fixed threshold (zero F1) — visible in the thermal table, where the GRU alone clears both bars. We report both metrics and treat threshold selection as an integral part of the monitoring problem.

The reported metric is matched to the objective in advance, by construction.

Conclusion & Impact

Match the architecture to the monitoring objective

Reliability monitoring becomes a tractable, decision-oriented machine-learning problem with a concrete rule: default to the GRU as generalist and incident detector, and switch to the Transformer for periodic signals. The degradation of otherwise strong models outside their domain of specialisation is a caution against single-metric benchmarking, and the low-rank manifold result hands operators one interpretable control parameter.

Limitations (stated candidly, from the paper)

  • The telemetry generator is physically motivated but remains a model, not measured device data; cross-domain validation uses standard NAB benchmarks as analogues, not quantum-calibration logs.
  • Absolute detection scores are modest, as expected for a rigorous, untuned benchmark — we make no state-of-the-art claim.
  • The deep sequence-model metrics are single seeded runs (point estimates), not multi-seed confidence intervals.

Outlook

  • Validate on archival calibration records from operating processors.
  • Add uncertainty-aware forecasting via Monte-Carlo dropout or deep ensembles.
  • Couple the forecaster to a recalibration policy under an explicit cost model.

Open, hardware-agnostic and quick to reproduce — a leakage-controlled, CPU-reproducible substrate for quantum-processor reliability research.
