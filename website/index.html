Objective-Aware Sequence Modelling for Drift Forecasting and Anomaly Detection in Quantum Hardware

We recast superconducting-qubit reliability as a multivariate, mean-reverting forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem and benchmark four sequence-model families under one leakage-free protocol: coherence is genuinely forecastable, and the best detector is objective-dependent — there is no universal winner. Background & Motivation

Why qubit-parameter drift is a sequence-modelling problem

Superconducting processors are not static. Relaxation time $T_1$, dephasing time $T_2$, gate fidelities, readout-assignment error, error-per-Clifford and tunable-coupling parameters all evolve under temperature, flux noise, two-level-system dynamics and recalibration history. In the NISQ regime this caps usable circuit depth; in the fault-tolerant regime it threatens the assumption that physical error rates stay below threshold long enough for error correction to suppress logical errors.

At hundred- to thousand-qubit scale, deciding when a device has drifted out of specification and which intervals warrant recalibration is a data problem beyond manual inspection. The calibration stream is a multivariate stochastic process with slow periodic structure, secular degradation and stochastic fluctuation — forecasting it and flagging off-manifold windows maps onto modern time-series learning. The gap: little decision-oriented guidance on which model family to deploy, at what parameter cost, for which objective.

Contributions & Novelty

Exactly what is new

The manuscript states three contributions; each is reproducible end-to-end on commodity CPU hardware.

  1. A leakage-free, objective-aware benchmark. We formalise quantum-hardware reliability as a joint forecasting-and-anomaly-detection problem over a physically motivated, reproducible telemetry generator, and release one leakage-free benchmark evaluating four sequence-model families on this task and on three heterogeneous real-world regimes.
  2. Model selection must be objective-aware. The GRU is a robust, parameter-efficient generalist — the only model to achieve non-trivial incident detection — whereas the Transformer is a specialist that excels on periodic calibration-like signals but fails to generalise. No single architecture wins everywhere.
  3. A reconstruction detector with a provable knob. We introduce a low-rank reconstruction-based drift detector and identify the bottleneck rank as the control knob separating drift from nominal behaviour — an interpretable, training-free-at-inference monitor backed by an empirical-optimality theorem.
Mathematics & Method

The model, the estimators, and why they work

Two theorems explain the two headline empirics: why forecast skill grows with horizon, and why detection is strongest at low reconstruction rank.

Drift generator (physics)

A mean-reverting multivariate telemetry process

For qubit $q$ at time $t$ (hours) the relaxation time superposes a 48-hour recalibration-like oscillation, secular ageing, and Gaussian fluctuation:

$$ T_1(q,t) = \max\!\Big(10,\; 80 + 20\sin\!\big(\tfrac{2\pi t}{48} + 0.8q\big) - 0.05\,t + \varepsilon\Big), \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2^2). $$

$T_2 = 0.9\,T_1\,\eta$ tracks $T_1$; the other channels are analogous bounded processes. A window is out-of-spec when $T_1 < 50~\mu\text{s}$ or single-qubit fidelity $< 0.998$. $Q=5$ qubits, $N=200$ half-hour steps (100 h), seven channels.

Leak-free windowing

Sliding windows, chronological splits

Series are segmented into windows of length $L=32$ predicting an $H=8$-step $T_1$ horizon, pooled across qubits. Leakage is prevented by chronological splitting: $70/15/15$ for the sequence-model benchmark, per-qubit $80/20$ for forecasting baselines, randomised $70/30$ for the detector (safe only because it is fit on nominal windows alone). Scalers are fit on training data and applied forward.

Ridge closed form

Multivariate ridge forecaster

With flattened windows as rows of $X$ and 8-step horizons as rows of $Y$, ridge regression solves $\min_W \lVert XW - Y\rVert_2^2 + \alpha\lVert W\rVert_2^2$ with the closed form ($\alpha=5$)

$$ W^\star = (X^\top X + \alpha I)^{-1} X^\top Y. $$

The $\alpha I$ term keeps the normal equations invertible despite correlated channels. Baselines: persistence, climatology, AR-ridge (32-step $T_1$ history only).

Horizon-skill theorem

Why skill widens with horizon

For a weakly stationary, zero-mean process with variance $\gamma_0$ and autocorrelation $\rho(h)$, persistence has risk $2\gamma_0(1-\rho(h))$ and the optimal single-lag predictor risk $\gamma_0(1-\rho(h)^2)$, so

$$ S(h) = 1 - \frac{R_\star(h)}{R_{\mathrm{pers}}(h)} = \frac{1-\rho(h)}{2}. $$

As $\rho(h)$ decays, $S(h)$ rises monotonically toward $\tfrac12$. This bound uses only the last sample; the multivariate ridge exceeds it by using the full 32-step, 7-channel history — measured skill reaches 72% at horizon 8.

PCA reconstruction detector

Drift as off-manifold residual energy

Fit the top-$k$ right singular vectors $V_k$ of the centred nominal matrix and project with $P_k = V_kV_k^\top$. The drift score $s_k(x) = \lVert x - xP_k\rVert_2^2$ is, by an empirical-optimality theorem (Rayleigh–Ritz), the smallest residual any rank-$k$ linear monitor fitted on nominal data can give. When $k = d$, $P_k = I$, the score is constant and detection collapses to chance — a theorem, not a tuning artefact.

ROC-AUC (Mann–Whitney)

The scale-free yardstick for detection

For independent drift $X^{+}$ and nominal $X^{-}$ windows, $\mathrm{AUC} = \Pr[s(X^{+}) > s(X^{-})] + \tfrac12\Pr[s(X^{+}) = s(X^{-})]$. AUC is invariant under any strictly increasing reparametrisation of $s$ — including per-feature affine rescaling — which is why it, not MAE/RMSE, is the basis for the cross-domain comparison.

Experimental Design & Statistics

How the numbers were produced

The benchmark separates replicated quantities from single-run point estimates and reports the metric matched to each objective.

Replication & uncertainty

What ± means here

Forecasting metrics are aggregated over n = 5 generator seeds; the reconstruction detector over n = 8 randomised splits. Uncertainties are mean ± standard deviation. The deep sequence-model metrics (thermal & cross-domain tables) are single deterministic, seeded runs reported as point estimates — stated plainly, not as confidence intervals.

Leakage control

Train/test windowing

Chronological splits keep future intervals out of training: $70/15/15$ for the sequence-model benchmark, per-qubit $80/20$ for baselines, randomised $70/30$ for the detector (fit on nominal windows only). Window length 32, horizon 8, pooled across five qubits.

Model families compared

Four sequence models + baselines

Single-layer Elman RNN, two-layer LSTM, two-layer GRU, encoder-only Transformer (pre-LN, $d_\text{model}=128$, 4 heads, 3 layers). Each emits an 8-step forecast and a drift logit, trained with AdamW under a cosine schedule on $\mathrm{MSE}+\mathrm{BCE}$. Forecasting baselines: persistence, climatology, AR-ridge, multivariate ridge.

ROC-AUC methodology

Threshold-free ranking

Detection is scored by F1 at the default threshold and threshold-free ROC-AUC (Mann–Whitney $U$). Both are reported because they disagree: a model can rank well (high AUC) yet detect nothing at a fixed operating point (zero F1). Cross-domain uses three NAB datasets as analogues for periodic, failure-bearing and irregular regimes.

Unsupervised drift detector

Low rank separates drift

Rank-3 reconstruction separates regimes with ROC-AUC 0.72 on a held-out split (0.70 ± 0.05 over 8 splits). Sweeping the bottleneck rank, detection is strongest at very low rank (0.87 ± 0.02 at rank 1) and collapses toward chance as the code becomes over-complete and memorises drifted windows.

Drift lives off a low-rank manifold; the rank is the false-alarm dial.
Key statistical comparison

F1 and ROC-AUC disagree by design

A model can rank intervals well (high ROC-AUC) yet detect nothing at a fixed threshold (zero F1) — visible in the thermal table, where only the GRU clears both. We report both and treat threshold selection as part of the monitoring problem.

Reported metric is matched to the objective, not chosen post-hoc.

Conclusion & Impact

Match the architecture to the monitoring objective

The work reframes reliability monitoring as a tractable, decision-oriented ML problem with an actionable rule: deploy the GRU as a default generalist and incident detector, reserve the Transformer for periodic signals. The failure of strong models outside their niche is a caution against single-metric benchmarking; the low-rank manifold result gives operators one interpretable dial.

Honest limitations (from the paper)

  • The telemetry generator is physically motivated but a model, not measured device data; cross-domain validation uses standard NAB benchmarks as analogues, not quantum-calibration logs.
  • Absolute detection scores are modest, as expected for an honest, untuned benchmark — no state-of-the-art claim is made.
  • Deep sequence-model metrics are single seeded runs (point estimates), not multi-seed confidence intervals.

Outlook

  • Validation on archival calibration records from operating processors.
  • Uncertainty-aware forecasting via Monte-Carlo dropout or deep ensembles.
  • Coupling the forecaster to a recalibration policy under an explicit cost model.

Open, hardware-agnostic and fast to reproduce — a leakage-controlled, CPU-reproducible substrate for quantum-processor reliability work.
